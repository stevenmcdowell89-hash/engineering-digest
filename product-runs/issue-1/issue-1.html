The month's biggest announcements were not about new value but new rails. Visa and Mastercard specified, on a single June day, who an agent may pay and which merchants are ready to be paid. The UK switched on its first new payment scheme since 2008. Apple taught the iPhone to ingest any loyalty card. None of these rails carries an offer yet — while at home, UK banks were re-gating the value they already distribute.

For the roadmap

Most of this month is context. Two threads are grounded in what shipped — or pointedly didn't.

  1. The A2A coverage gap is now operational. Card-linked offers read card transactions; the recurring payments collecting under UKPI's new scheme don't produce one. → Trend 02
  2. Loyalty is absent from the agent rails. The networks' new agentic trust layer specifies verified agents, merchant readiness and spending credentials — and no offers or rewards object. → Trend 01
01
Trend · Agentic commerce
Agentic commerce builds its trust layer

On 10 June both card networks shipped the trust layer for agent-led commerce — scores, directories and credentials that will decide who may transact on a customer's behalf. Offers and rewards appear nowhere in it.

Visa specifies agent readiness

At its Payments Forum, Visa announced Agent Score — a rating of how prepared a merchant is to receive agent-initiated traffic — alongside an Agentic Directory of verified agents and merchants, and a Large Transaction Model for intelligent commerce. Together they sketch the layer that decides which counterparties exist, and in what standing, when software does the shopping.

Source: Business Wire (Visa)

Visa credentials go inside OpenAI's checkout

The same day, Visa and OpenAI announced a partnership to embed Visa payments across OpenAI's agentic commerce experiences — putting network credentials directly into ChatGPT-scale purchasing, where the agent rather than the human sees the checkout.

Source: Visa Newsroom

Mastercard launches Agent Pay for Machines

Mastercard answered with AP4M: credentialed, permissioned machine-to-machine payments with 30-plus partners including Stripe, Adyen and Checkout.com, settling across cards, accounts and stablecoins. It anticipates spend that is high-frequency and micro-value — paced by machines, not by people choosing a card.

Source: Mastercard Newsroom · The Paypers

What it looks like in product →

The agentic stack now specifies verified agent identities, merchant-readiness scores, spending permissions and multi-rail settlement. None of the three releases mentions offers, rewards or loyalty. The layer that will decide what an agent can see and weigh at checkout is being written this year, and the category's core objects are not yet defined in it.

Sources: Visa · Mastercard

Trend · A2A / Pay by Bank
Pay by Bank goes operational

UKPI's commercial VRP scheme switches on

The UK Payments Initiative took its variable-recurring-payments scheme live with a single commercial rulebook across 31 firms, including all the major retail banks. Wave 1 covers regulated and trusted sectors; Wave 2 extends to e-commerce in the second half of 2026. The FCA called it out as a milestone for open banking at national scale.

Source: FCA · Open Banking Expo

GoCardless aims Recurring Pay by Bank at the card rails

On scheme day one, GoCardless launched Recurring Pay by Bank, positioning it explicitly against the card networks' roughly £1.5bn annual UK fee take. Subscription and repeat spend — the natural territory of retention offers — is the first target.

Source: GoCardless

TrueLayer's Bank on File carries the scheme's first live payments

TrueLayer launched Bank on File — a stored-credential relationship for bank payments, authorised biometrically, replacing the card on file — with Trading 212, IG Group, InvestEngine and East Lothian Housing Association already live. It cites card failure rates of 5–10% in subscription billing as the wedge.

Source: IT Brief UK

What it looks like in product →

A card-linked offer needs a card transaction to read; a Pay-by-Bank payment doesn't produce one. Bank on File's live merchants are collecting recurring payments today that sit outside card-linked measurement, and Wave 2 brings e-commerce onto the same rails within months. The UK context matters: consumer interchange here has been capped for years and offers are already merchant-funded — this is not a US-style funding story, it is a visibility one.

Sources: FCA · IT Brief UK

03
Trend · Surfaces
The OS wallet bids for loyalty

This month the phone's wallet moved from a place loyalty cards can live to a surface that actively recruits them — and the card credential underneath grew more flexible too.

iOS 27 ingests any loyalty card and presents it by tap

At WWDC, Apple showed iOS 27's Wallet scanning any physical loyalty card into the phone by camera, and Tap to Share passing loyalty membership to a merchant's terminal in-store. Loyalty identity becomes an OS-level object, acquired and redeemed without a retailer app or a banking app in the loop. The features ship with iOS 27 in September.

Source: MacRumors · Apple Newsroom

Visa Flexible Credential reaches the UK

On 12 May — the day before this issue's window opens — Visa's Flexible Credential went live in the UK on Zilch cards with processor Thredd: one credential carrying multiple funding options that can be, in Visa's words, financed, optimised and rewarded in real time. Where rewards logic attaches to a UK card is becoming a design choice, not a given.

Source: Visa Newsroom UK

Why it matters

The banking app's claim to be the natural home of offers rests on being where the card lives. When the OS wallet can ingest any loyalty card, present loyalty identity at the till, and the credential itself can re-route funding and rewards in real time, that claim is contested from both above and below — the surface where an offer is seen and redeemed is now a competitive variable, not a fixed fact about the channel.

04
Trend · Rewards mechanics
Rewards, re-gated

The UK's biggest banking brands re-answered the same question this month — what is a reward actually for — and gave three different answers: a first-party offers hub, gated cashback, and unconditional cash.

Lloyds builds the offers hub in-house

Lloyds launched Lloyds Rewards, an in-app hub across Lloyds, Halifax and Bank of Scotland: monthly prize draws of £2,026 for 50 customers — entry gated on £1,000-plus deposited in the month — alongside point-of-purchase merchant offers (Smol, Virgin Wines, Readly) and personalised cashback at brands including Sainsbury's, B&M and IHG. The first draw opened 1 June. The UK's largest retail banking group is now running merchant-funded, personalised offers as a first-party product.

Source: Lloyds Banking Group · Brit Brief

Chase doubles cashback, but makes it conditional on behaviour

Chase UK's cashback rises to 2% (monthly cap up from £15 to £20) — but from 1 June, qualifying requires 15 card transactions or Direct Debit payments a month, plus £1,000 held continuously in Chase savings. The reward now pays for engagement and balances rather than mere presence. It is the clearest UK example yet of cashback being repriced as an engagement mechanic.

Source: MoneySavingExpert · criteria change

Nationwide writes 4.4 million cheques instead

At its 21 May results, Nationwide confirmed another £100 member payment — £440m to 4.4 million members, landing 10–30 June. No merchant funding, no redemption journey, no targeting: a flat cash dividend for holding the right products. It is the opposite theory of loyalty value, and it keeps winning headlines.

Source: Nationwide · MoneySavingExpert

Why it matters

These are the benchmarks any merchant-funded offers proposition is now measured against inside a UK bank: a first-party offers hub at the country's largest retail bank, engagement-gated cashback that earns its cost in activity, and unconditional cash that earns its cost in goodwill. The Lloyds move cuts both ways — it validates the category's economics at the largest possible scale, and it shows the biggest buyer's build-it-in-house option is no longer hypothetical.
