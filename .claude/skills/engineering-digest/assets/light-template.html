The Leadership Read

The Art of the Feedback Conversation

Lara Hogan — former VP of Engineering at Kickstarter, author of Resilient Management, and one of the clearest thinkers on engineering leadership — wrote extensively about how to structure feedback conversations that actually change behaviour. Her framework has become a reference point across the industry, and it's worth revisiting.

The equation: observation + impact + question

Hogan's core model strips feedback down to three components. Start with a specific observation — what you saw, not your interpretation of it. Follow with the impact — what effect it had on the team, the project, or the person's own goals. Then ask a question — opening space for the other person's perspective rather than delivering a verdict. The structure prevents feedback from becoming personal ("you're bad at this") or vague ("you need to communicate better") by grounding it in observable facts.

Why engineering managers get this wrong

The most common failure mode Hogan identifies is the "feedback sandwich" — positive, negative, positive — which she argues trains people to distrust praise and brace for criticism. The second failure is saving feedback for performance reviews, by which point the specific context has faded and the conversation becomes about patterns rather than moments. The third is conflating feedback with instruction: telling someone what to do differently isn't feedback, it's direction. Feedback creates awareness; what the person does with that awareness is where growth happens.

Feedback is most useful at the lowest level of abstraction. The more specific you can be, the more actionable it becomes.

— Lara Hogan

The timing question

Hogan advocates for feedback delivered within 48 hours of the observation — close enough that both parties remember the context, far enough that initial emotional reactions have settled. She also distinguishes between feedback that should be private (anything that could feel like criticism) and feedback that can be public (specific, genuine praise — but only if the person is comfortable with public recognition, which not everyone is).

Why this still matters

In an environment where AI tools are changing what engineering work looks like week to week, the ability to give clear, specific feedback on new kinds of work — prompt quality, agent orchestration decisions, knowing when to trust and when to verify — becomes more important, not less. The fundamentals don't change: be specific, be timely, be curious. But the situations you're giving feedback on are evolving fast.

Outside In

How Wise Builds Payment Infrastructure Across 80+ Countries

Wise (formerly TransferWise) published an engineering blog detailing how they've built a payment infrastructure platform that operates across more than 80 countries with different regulatory frameworks, banking systems, and payment rails. For anyone in the payments or loyalty space, the architectural decisions are directly relevant.

The core challenge

Every country has different payment infrastructure — different clearing systems, different settlement cycles, different compliance requirements. Wise's approach is to abstract these differences behind a unified internal API, so product teams build against a single interface regardless of which country's payment rails they're using underneath. The abstraction layer handles routing, compliance checks, settlement timing, and failure modes per-country.

What's interesting about the architecture

Wise runs each country's payment integration as an independent service with its own deployment lifecycle. If the Brazilian payment rail integration needs updating, it can be deployed without touching the UK or EU integrations. They call this "corridor independence" — the ability to ship changes to one payment corridor without risking others. It's a form of blast radius control applied to financial infrastructure rather than traditional microservices.

They also maintain what they call "payment route scoring" — a real-time system that evaluates the reliability, cost, and speed of available payment routes and automatically selects the optimal path. If a bank connection degrades, the system routes around it before customers notice.

Why this matters

The pattern of abstracting country-specific complexity behind a unified interface is applicable beyond payments. Any organisation operating across multiple regulatory jurisdictions — banking, insurance, fintech — faces the same tension between local compliance requirements and engineering simplicity. Wise's answer is to make the complexity explicit and contained rather than letting it leak across the codebase.
