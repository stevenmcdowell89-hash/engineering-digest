GitLab Redraws the Org Chart for the Agentic Era (4 min)

Jargon Watch Agentic era · team topology · RIF

Agentic era — the framing that AI agents will carry out routine engineering and operational work directed by people, rather than only assisting a human keystroke by keystroke.

Team topology — the deliberate design of how many teams exist, how big they are, and where the boundaries between them sit. Changing it changes who owns what.

RIF — reduction in force; the corporate term for a layoff or headcount cut.

GitLab announced its "Act 2" restructure on 11 May and detailed the financial scope alongside Q1 results in early June; the reaction has carried into this week as peers read it as a template. What is new now is less the headline cut than the org shape underneath it — a deliberate redesign of team topology built around agents doing internal work.

What actually changed

CEO Bill Staples is cutting roughly 7% of staff, removing up to three layers of management in some functions, and reducing the number of countries GitLab operates small teams in by up to 30%. The structural move is the one to watch: the research-and-development organisation is being broken into about 60 smaller, more autonomous teams — close to a doubling of the number of independent teams.

BeforeLayered R&D, up to 3 management tiers between IC and exec
FlattenUp to 3 management layers removed in some functions
SplitR&D broken into ~60 small autonomous teams
AutomateInternal agents run reviews, approvals & handoffs

The bet underneath the cut

The agents in question are the assistive end of the spectrum, not money-moving autonomy: GitLab plans to deploy them internally to automate reviews, approvals, and handoffs — the connective tissue that management layers traditionally exist to coordinate. The logic is that if agents carry the coordination load, fewer humans are needed to sit between the people doing the work and the people setting direction. Smaller teams with more authority, fewer layers above them.

"The new unit of delivery is a small autonomous team — and GitLab is betting it can run roughly sixty of them where it used to run far fewer, larger ones."

— Engineering Digest, on GitLab's Act 2 restructure

Why it lands beyond GitLab

The "agentic era" framing is doing a lot of justifying across the sector — Cloudflare cut around 1,100 roles with a similar pivot, and 2026 has logged more than 95,000 tech job losses across some 247 events. Treated cynically, the framing is cover for a cut. Treated seriously, GitLab is one of the first named devtools vendors to publish a concrete answer to a question many engineering leaders are now being asked: if agents take on internal coordination, how many management layers does the org actually fund, and what size should a team be? GitLab's answer — flatter, smaller, more numerous — is now a public reference point for that conversation, whatever one makes of the headcount maths.

The Trust Layer for Agent-to-Agent Payments Goes Live

Jargon Watch Agentic commerce · AP4M · agentic tokens

Agentic commerce — transactions where an AI agent completes a purchase or settlement on a person's behalf, within limits they set, without approving each step.

AP4M — Mastercard's Agent Pay for Machines: a network for permissioning, orchestrating and settling those agent-initiated payments.

Agentic tokens / tokenisation — replacing a real card number with a one-time credential scoped to an agent and its spending rules, so the agent never holds the live card details.

On 10 June Mastercard launched Agent Pay for Machines, a network to permission and settle payments made by AI agents across cards, bank accounts and stablecoin rails. More than 30 partners are listed at launch — Adyen, Stripe, Coinbase, Cloudflare, OKX and Checkout.com among them — with agent permissions initially recorded on blockchains including Polygon, Solana and Base.

These are production agents with action authority: they move real money, at very high volume, very small value and very low latency. The same week, Visa announced an OpenAI tie-up putting tokenised Visa credentials behind agent checkout inside ChatGPT and Codex, with spending limits enforced before any payment authorises. Two of the largest networks are racing to be the trust-and-consent layer for machine-initiated spend — and both are anchoring it in tokenisation rather than raw card numbers.

For a fintech in the loyalty and commerce-media patch, this is the infrastructure the next set of customer conversations will reference — the world it operates in is getting an agent-payments layer whether or not any single product touches it yet.

The takeaway: the trust-and-consent layer for agent-initiated payments is becoming real infrastructure this quarter — the conversation bank customers will open is how a loyalty or commerce-media stack handles tokenised, agent-initiated transactions, not whether they will happen.

The Middle-Management Squeeze Is Now a Pattern

Meta began company-wide layoffs on 20 May, cutting around 8,000 roles — roughly 10% of its workforce — with more than 1,400 management positions eliminated, close to a third of the total, per reporting from Reuters carried by multiple outlets. Management and software-engineering roles were hit hardest as spend shifts toward an AI capital budget Meta puts at $125–145bn for 2026.

Read alongside GitLab's layer-flattening and Atlassian's earlier reshaping, the single-company number matters less than the shape: the management tier is where several large orgs are choosing to cut first, with AI cited as the reason.

The takeaway: the middle-management squeeze is a sector pattern now, not one company — it changes how leaders justify the number of management layers they fund and how they plan org shape for the next cycle.

Sources: LatestLY, Yahoo Finance (Reuters)

What LDX3 Revealed About Interviewing When Candidates Have AI

Stephane Moreau · blog4EMs, reporting from LeadingEng / LDX3 2026 · Read the full piece

Moreau's recap of the LDX3 2026 conference circles a problem every hiring loop now shares: candidates have AI in the room, so the old "can you write this function" interview tests the wrong thing. The most concrete account came from Meta's Danit Nativ Navon, who described rebuilding the interview rather than banning the tool. Meta now hands a candidate a full codebase and explicitly permits AI assistance, then assesses what does not come from the model — how the candidate explores a problem before diving in, and how they validate and question the AI's output rather than accepting it. Meta ran 9,000 interviews in that format before rolling it out fully in April 2026.

Nicole Forsgren gave the loop a vocabulary. She named three kinds of friction that AI amplifies rather than removes: velocity friction (slow builds and deploys), cognitive friction (unclear criteria, constant context-switching, nobody sure what "done" means), and knowledge friction (engineers unable to reach architectural intent or understand adjacent systems). The point for an interviewer: AI flatters velocity, so a hiring bar that only measures output speed will miss the candidates who reduce the other two.

Assess how they validate and question the AI's output — not whether they can produce it.

Wise's Rick Clegg closed the loop on the other side of the hire. His developer-experience team built workflow-integrated AI tools — a code-flow visualiser, a PR-review assistant — aimed at legibility, helping engineers understand systems, rather than at raw speed. The thread running through all three: in the agent era the durable signal is judgement about machine output, and that holds whether you are designing an interview or the tools the new hire will use on day one.

What to try The next interview-loop review is a chance to ask which stage still rewards typing speed over judgement — Meta rebuilt theirs to assess how candidates interrogate the model's output.

Datadog Makes the Postmortem a Number Leadership Can Read

DD
Datadog DASH 2026 feature roundup · Operating at scale June 2026
Read the full post →

"Did we actually finish the postmortem?" is a question most engineering leaders can only answer by asking around. At its DASH 2026 conference, Datadog turned that question into a metric. A postmortem (the written review after an incident — what broke, why, and what changes) can now be set to Draft, In Review, or Completed, with a named owner — often the incident commander — accountable for driving it to done.

From assertion to facet

The structural move is exposing that lifecycle data as incident facets — filterable attributes on every incident. That lets leadership report on postmortem coverage across the whole org: the percentage of SEV-1 incidents (the most severe tier, a major customer-facing outage) that have a completed postmortem becomes a dashboard number rather than a claim made in a meeting.

The shift is from reliability maturity as assertion — "we always do postmortems" — to reliability maturity as data leadership can actually see and trend. Once "what fraction of our serious incidents get a finished review?" has a number, it stops being a matter of culture-by-reputation and starts being something a director can manage.

The tension worth noticing

This cuts against a thread the digest ran recently: Issue 14's Outside In featured Honeycomb's Lex Neva arguing teams should drop rigid action-item tracking because it ossifies into compliance theatre. Datadog is, in effect, making postmortem completion more trackable. Both can be right — the question is whether the metric measures learning or just paperwork, and that depends entirely on what completion is allowed to mean.

The takeaway: when reliability maturity becomes a reportable number, it gets managed like one — the leadership judgement is making sure the metric rewards genuine learning, not just a box marked "completed."

The Leadership Read

The Engineering-Manager Job Is Splitting in Two (5 min)

SM
Stephane Moreau Senior Engineering Manager, Zopa Bank · LeadDev June 2026
Read on LeadDev →

Stephane Moreau's argument is blunt: the single job title "engineering manager" is fracturing into two roles that need different people, and most career ladders haven't caught up. As agent-assisted output lets teams ship more with fewer people, the org's appetite for management changes — and the EM role splits along the line between staying technical and scaling scope.

The two roles hiding inside one title

On one side is the tech-lead-manager (a TLM — a manager who stays hands-on technically while running a single small team). On the other is the multi-team manager who oversees broader scope across several product areas with little day-to-day technical depth. Moreau's point is that these are not two rungs of one ladder; they are diverging tracks, and pretending one naturally leads to the other sets people up to fail in a job they never chose.

Span of control is the lever

The structural pressure shows up as span of control — how many people report to one manager. Moreau cites Meta's new applied-AI engineering team reportedly running at a 50:1 employee-to-manager ratio, far past the single-digit numbers most research recommends. He sets that against a Happily.ai review of 200-plus companies finding that managers with 15 or more direct reports score about 20% lower on team engagement than those with seven or fewer. The two numbers frame the trade-off: agent-era efficiency pushes spans wider, while the engagement evidence pushes them narrower.

50:1
Reported employee-to-manager ratio, Meta applied-AI team — vs a ~20% engagement drop above 15 reports

The traditional EM role was built for a team shape that may not survive the decade.

— Stephane Moreau, LeadDev

Why it matters past Meta's scale

Few orgs will run at 50:1, and that is rather the point: the interesting decision is no longer drift. When AI changes how much one team can deliver, the span-of-control number and the choice between a TLM track and a scope track become deliberate design calls. Moreau's contribution is naming the split clearly enough that a leadership team can choose which model to fund — and build a ladder that promotes people into the role they're actually suited to, rather than defaulting everyone toward "more reports equals more seniority."

This could be worth talking about: Engineering leaders are now choosing a span-of-control model on purpose rather than letting it drift — which of the two manager tracks does the current ladder actually reward, and is that the one the org needs as agents take on more of the work?
