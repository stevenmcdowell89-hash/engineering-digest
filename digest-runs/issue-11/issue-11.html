Lead

Meta cuts 8,000 jobs — the manager-of-managers layer compresses

The 4 AM cut

At 4 AM Singapore time on Tuesday, Meta engineers found their corporate accounts suspended. By close of business in Menlo Park, the company had confirmed roughly 8,000 cuts — the largest engineering layoff round of 2026 — with another ~6,000 open requisitions cancelled. Reality Labs (Meta's AR/VR division) and Facebook social took the heaviest blow on the product side. Recruiting and sales operations were trimmed across the board. And the layer that absorbed a disproportionate share of cuts, multiple secondary reports confirm, was middle management: directors, senior engineering managers, and the manager-of-managers tier whose teams were judged duplicative under what the company's leadership described as a flatter, AI-native operating model.

Where the compression lands

The single round puts Meta's cut at roughly 7% of all 2026 tech-sector layoffs to date. Cumulative numbers across the year now stand at 179 events and 113,863 workers, according to industry aggregators. The shape of the round is doing the structural work. Non-AI product engineering teams were thinned. AI-native and infrastructure roles were largely preserved or actively expanded against the company's ongoing $125–145bn capex plan. The implication is structural: Meta is rebalancing the org around fewer coordination layers and more direct execution capacity, with AI tooling cited as the lever that makes the new ratio defensible.

Data: CNBC, NPR, layoff aggregator totals as of 20 May 2026.

"Operate with a flatter structure with smaller teams of pods and cohorts that can move faster and with more ownership."

— Janelle Gale, Meta Chief People Officer, internal memo (20 May 2026)

The conversation peers are having

For engineering leaders at peer fintechs and banks — many of whom share talent markets with Meta's London and Singapore footprints — this is a tier-1 industry signal landing in the middle of Q2 levelling and team-shape conversations. The substance of the conversation has shifted from "what AI tools do we buy" to "what does the manager-of-managers layer cost, and what does it produce that the layer below can't?" Meta has chosen its answer publicly. The Wise, Monzo, Revolut, and bank-peer engineering organisations now have a reference data point for theirs.

The honest version is narrower than the headline. Meta has not eliminated middle management; it has compressed it. Directors with thin spans of control, recruiting partners whose workflows AI tooling is absorbing, and product teams shipping into mature surfaces are the named categories. The categories preserved — AI engineering, infrastructure, the senior IC layer running model-adjacent work — map onto the operating model the rest of the industry has been quietly drifting toward all year.

The takeaway: Meta has handed every Q2 org-design conversation a fresh reference point. The question on the table at peer fintechs and banks this week is no longer whether AI restructuring touches the manager-of-managers layer — it is which directors, which sub-teams, and which coordination layers can defend their cost against a comparator that just published its answer.

Sources: CNBC, NPR, HCAmag, Kore1 (May 2026)

Railway's GCP suspension turns a billing decision into a live concentration-risk story

What happened

At 22:20 UTC on 19 May, Google Cloud incorrectly suspended Railway's production billing account. The platform went dark for paying customers across regions and stayed dark until roughly 06:14 UTC on 20 May — about eight hours of total outage from a single vendor-side billing decision. Railway's own services were healthy throughout; the underlying compute simply stopped accepting traffic for a tenant the cloud provider had flagged.

Why it spread

The blast radius (the surface area of impact from a single failure) extended past the GCP boundary. Network paths cached at peers and CDN edges continued routing to the suspended account until those paths expired through their normal TTLs. The recovery path was outside Railway's hands — it required a vendor reversal first, then waiting for the cached routes to clear.

Single-account architectureMulti-account / multi-cloud
One billing decision = total outageSuspension blast radius bounded to one tenant
Cached routes propagate impact beyond the cloud boundaryOther accounts continue to serve traffic
Recovery dependent on vendor response timeFailover under engineering team's control

The takeaway: Railway's incident hands every architecture-review meeting this quarter a clean reference case for single-vendor concentration risk — the kind of conversation that's been theoretical until it isn't.

Banking, fintech & payments
Jargon Watch MPP & SPT

Machine Payments Protocol (MPP) — an open standard for transactions initiated by AI agents acting on a human's behalf, co-authored by Stripe and Tempo. Defines how an agent identifies itself, proves authorisation, and settles a payment.

Shared Payment Token (SPT) — Stripe's token primitive that abstracts the underlying funding source (card, bank, stablecoin). MPP uses SPTs as the settlement instrument.

Stripe and Tempo's Machine Payments Protocol gathers momentum

What MPP is

Stripe's Machine Payments Protocol — announced at Sessions 2026 in late April and continuing to gather industry attention this week — is an open standard for agent-initiated transactions, co-authored with Tempo. MPP defines how AI agents identify themselves and settle programmatic transactions through Shared Payment Tokens, with settlement available in card rails, bank rails, or stablecoins. Named early users include Browserbase (agent browser sessions), PostalForm (physical mail), Parallel Web Systems (per-API-call web access), and Stripe Climate (programmatic carbon contributions).

Why this lands now

The protocol ships as an open standard rather than a Stripe-proprietary integration. That detail changes the architecture conversation bracketing every commerce-media and loyalty stack. Whether to track MPP as a serious integration target — this year, next year, or never — becomes a real platform decision now that the protocol exists for other rails and providers to align around.

The takeaway: an open agent-commerce protocol is the substrate the bank-customer base may shortly be asked to support. The architecture conversation has moved from speculative to specific.

Sources: Stripe newsroom, Sessions 2026 recap

Expectations for an Engineering Manager at Monzo

DM
Diogo Matias Senior Engineering Manager · Monzo September 2025
Concept of the week Manager of managers

The layer above front-line engineering managers — typically a director or senior EM whose direct reports are themselves managers. The transition into the role is one of the harder shifts in engineering leadership: less day-to-day delivery, more pattern-recognition across teams, calibration consistency, and developing other managers as the craft.

Matias opens with a line that does most of the section's structural work: "make bad news flow much faster than good news." Written by a manager of managers to the EMs reporting into him, the piece reads as a set of expectations published deliberately, in plain language — what he is looking for, what he is not. It is the canonical archetype for this slot for a reason: it is operational, it is from a named operator at a real bank, and every section is one transferable claim.

The substance is a numbered set of expectations covering how an EM drives their area: setting and defending direction, delivering impact through the team rather than around it, building psychological safety as a delivery prerequisite (not a wellbeing programme), holding a high hiring and feedback bar, and demonstrating the manager-of-managers behaviour itself — coaching other managers, surfacing patterns across teams, calibrating consistently with peers. The expectations are written down so an EM can read them, score themselves, and ask Matias the awkward questions about gaps. The artefact does the structural work of compressing months of implicit calibration into a document.

The sharpest of the expectations is the bad-news-velocity one. The test of a healthy team, in Matias's framing, is whether problems arrive in his inbox before the dashboards confirm them — not after. The mechanism for that test is psychological safety, and the failure mode is a team that has learned to filter upwards rather than escalate. Bad news flowing faster than good news is the diagnostic for whether the manager-of-managers layer is actually doing its job, or simply absorbing summaries from below.

The transferable claim — particularly relevant to the Meta-cuts conversation opening this issue — is that the manager-of-managers layer is defensible when it produces signal the layer below cannot, and indefensible when it operates as a filter on signal the layer below already had. Matias's expectations are one bank engineering org's attempt to make that distinction concrete enough to hold a person to.

What to try Before the next manager-of-managers 1:1, pick one of Matias's expectations and consider which EM in the team would score lowest on it — and whether that gap is one they can see from where they're standing.

Outside In

Linear's reliability bar — and what it costs to hold it

L
Linear Engineering · Linear 2026

Linear has spent 2026 doing something most of the AI-feature-shipping cohort hasn't: not shipping. The company added AI-assisted triage only after meeting an explicit internal reliability bar, declined to bolt agent-mode (a tool primitive where the AI executes multi-step tasks without per-step human approval) into the core issue flow, and continues to ship under the same craft discipline that defined its first five years. The product positioning — "the issue tracker engineering teams actually choose" — depends on that discipline holding while peers are racing.

The reliability bar

Linear treats the bar as a constraint on shipping: a measurable threshold below which the team refuses to release a feature, set against the invariants the product is meant to protect. The invariants here are speed, predictability, and a UI that doesn't surprise the user. AI features that risk any of the three are held back until they meet the bar — not iterated in front of customers. The published positioning makes the trade-off plain: Linear would rather be six months behind on AI features than ship something that erodes the product's reliability narrative. The internal cost of that posture is real — engineering hours spent on instrumentation, additional review cycles, deferred launches — and the company has chosen to absorb it rather than dilute the bar.

A reliability bar is a constraint, and the constraint is the leadership work. Defining the bar takes an afternoon. Holding it when the competitive pressure to ship faster is loudest takes years. Linear's published answer is that the bar costs roughly the velocity of one feature track per quarter, and the company treats that cost as a fixed input to the operating model rather than a variable to optimise away.

The lesson for engineering leaders

For UK banking and fintech engineering organisations operating under their own reliability bar — regulatory plus customer-trust, both binding — Linear is the cleanest counterpoint to the rush-to-ship narrative running through the rest of this week's news. The substitution test holds: a large engineering org slowed AI-feature rollout to meet a reliability bar, absorbed the velocity cost, and made the discipline visible in its product positioning. The lesson lands without needing Linear's specific stack or product.

The takeaway: Linear's restraint is the operating discipline that protects the product the rest of the issue is shipping into — a useful reference point for any engineering org that has been quietly wondering whether its own reliability bar would survive a quarter under MPP, agent-mode, and a board asking why the AI features are six months behind.

The Leadership Read

The next era of observability: a leadership-craft framing

CM
Charity Majors & Christine Yen Co-founders · Honeycomb February 2026

Honeycomb's founders use their ten-year retrospective Q&A to argue that the leadership move, a decade into the observability era, is articulating what should actually be true about a system — defining the invariants a service is meant to protect, in language that survives nondeterministic behaviour, model drift, and cost-per-interaction pressure. The piece predates this week but the framing lands directly on the conversations DORA's ROI of AI report has reopened.

What changed

The starting point is honest. The "three pillars" framing — logs, metrics, traces — was always a vendor-shaped story; what engineering organisations actually needed was a way to ask questions of production behaviour. The next era, Majors and Yen argue, is shaped by two structural changes. First, software is increasingly nondeterministic by design — AI inference, retries, agents acting on partial state — so historical "alert on this threshold" patterns degrade into noise. Second, every user interaction now carries a measurable inference cost, so observability becomes part of unit economics rather than only a reliability discipline. Dashboards built for the deterministic era answer aggregate threshold questions; the team needs to understand why a specific interaction behaved the way it did — a different question, served by different instrumentation.

The leadership move

The piece's central move is from tooling to constraints. Nondeterministic systems generate behaviour that unfolds over time and cost, so the leader's job becomes defining the invariants (the properties of the system that must always hold true, regardless of state) the team is protecting and the constraints under which the system has to operate. Wide events (a single rich record per interaction capturing many dimensions, distinct from separate logs / metrics / traces) become the substrate for that work because they let the team ask new questions rather than only the ones the dashboard was built for.

"Static dashboards are indeed still a lie. Still useless. Becoming ever uselesser."

— Charity Majors, Honeycomb (February 2026)

Why this lands for engineering leaders

This pairs cleanly with the DORA ROI of AI report covered elsewhere in this issue: DORA's "system" — the engineering organisation underneath the team — is exactly the layer of constraints and invariants Majors and Yen are pointing at. A team with that clarity defines what good looks like before the agents start producing output, and verification becomes a check against a written-down invariant rather than a judgement call in a PR review. For the manager-of-managers layer compressing across the rest of this issue, the practical implication is that defending the layer's value is easier when the team can point to the invariants the layer maintains than when it can only point to the dashboards it watches.

The honest limit is worth naming. Honeycomb is a vendor in this space and the piece does not pretend otherwise. The leadership claim survives the commercial framing because it does not require Honeycomb's product — only a team that has written down what its services should do, what the cost ceiling per interaction is, and which failure modes warrant an alert. Most engineering organisations have not. That gap is the discipline the next era asks the leadership layer to close.
