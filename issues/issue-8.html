Foreword

The pilots are over. In April, OpenAI shipped Workspace Agents, Snowflake shipped Cortex Code, Microsoft shipped Agent Framework v1.0, and Lloyds Banking Group committed to extracting more than £100m of value from agentic AI in 2026. JPMorgan now scores its 65,000 engineers on how often they reach for Copilot. Banking has decided agents are infrastructure, not experiment.

The deployment numbers are real. The ROI numbers are not yet. This issue takes the entire week to look at why those two facts are sitting in the same room — what the latest Writer survey says, what UK banks are actually building, what the Bank of England is now treating as a supervisory priority, and what the productivity research says when you stop reading the headlines.

Three datasets, one tension. Writer.com surveyed 800 enterprise leaders and found that almost everyone has deployed AI agents in the past year, but fewer than a quarter can point to significant business returns. AI Magicx and Tyk's banking-specific data shows finance teams jumped from less than 7% agent adoption in January 2025 to 44% by Q1 2026, a six-fold increase in twelve months. And on April 1, the Bank of England and the PRA wrote jointly to the Chancellor naming AI as a supervisory priority for 2026 — with agentic AI explicitly flagged as the next item on the radar.

What follows takes each thread in turn. Writer's data on why deployment is not translating to ROI. The UK banking lean-in, anchored by Lloyds' £100m commitment and JPMorgan's internal Copilot scoreboards. The regulator's framing, reading existing rules onto a technology stack that is moving faster than the supervisory cycle. A Counterpoint on the developer-productivity research that complicates the simple "agents make us faster" narrative. And a case study on what JPMorgan's tracking infrastructure tells us about how the largest engineering organisations are operationalising the transition.

The pattern by the end is consistent. The gap between deployment and durable value is not a question of more time. It is a question of which engineering and operating decisions get made now — about validation, about pipeline architecture, about review cycles, about governance — that determine whether the spend compounds or evaporates.

Source 1 — Writer 2026 Enterprise AI Adoption Survey

Deployment is universal. Returns are not.

The headline number that matters

Writer's annual survey of 800 enterprise leaders, published April 7, captures the cleanest version of the central tension in agentic AI right now. Ninety-seven percent of executives report their company has deployed AI agents in the past year. Twenty-three percent say they are seeing significant ROI from those agents. The deployment metric is essentially saturated. The value metric is not.

That gap is not new in shape — it shows up in every enterprise tech adoption cycle — but the magnitude is unusual. Writer also reports that 59% of companies are now investing at least $1m annually in AI tooling. Sixty-seven percent of executives believe their company has already suffered a data breach due to unapproved AI tools. Sixty-nine percent are planning layoffs they attribute, at least in part, to AI.

Where the value is actually flowing

The minority who report significant ROI share four characteristics in Writer's data. They tie agents directly to revenue outcomes rather than productivity proxies. They architect platforms that give business teams autonomy while IT retains oversight. They put governance in place before scaling, not after. And they treat AI adoption as organisational redesign rather than technology rollout.

The breach number is the one to flag

The 67% data-breach figure is the line in this survey that matters most for a banking engineering leader. Writer's question is loose — "believe their company has already suffered" rather than "have confirmed and disclosed" — so the number should be read as an exposure signal rather than an incident count. But even read conservatively, two out of three executives think unapproved AI usage has already cost them. That is the line a CISO has to engage with, and it is the line that the BoE and PRA were obviously thinking about when they named AI a 2026 supervisory priority three weeks earlier.

Writer's CEO May Habib offered the verdict directly: "Layoffs are not a viable AI strategy." The companies extracting value, in her framing, are redesigning operations with human-agent collaboration in the design, not bolting agents onto an unchanged org chart and expecting the spreadsheet to fix itself.

Lloyds: from £50m to £100m+ in twelve months

In its 2025 results announcement on January 29, Lloyds Banking Group disclosed that generative AI delivered around £50m of value during 2025 and committed to "more than £100m" of additional value in 2026 — driven specifically by scaling agentic AI across the group. The Group is launching an AI Academy for 67,000 colleagues and committing to "significant strategic investments in agentic AI" for its 28 million customers. A full customer rollout of an AI-powered financial assistant is on the same 2026 milestone list.

Lloyds' framing matters because it is the most public commitment yet from a UK high-street bank that agentic AI is a P&L line, not a research budget. The numbers also matter: a 2x year-on-year value step, disclosed to shareholders, with a publicly stated target tied to specific use cases. The bank has effectively bet that its 2025 GenAI work was the warm-up.

JPMorgan: the dashboard era

On April 23, Business Insider published an investigation into how JPMorgan is operationalising its push. The bank has built internal dashboards that score each of its roughly 65,000 Global Technology engineers on AI tool usage — Copilot adoption, Claude usage, spending per user, total spend — visible to thousands of their peers. Engineers have been told to demonstrate "meaningful improvement" in both code quality and code volume by using AI. As Claude Code seats began rolling out to a wider population in early April, screenshots show a dedicated tracking dashboard already covering 600-plus engineers.

The bank says the dashboard isn't performance management

JPMorgan's spokesperson told Business Insider the data is not used in "performance management" and instead tracks the effectiveness of the bank's AI investment. That distinction will be tested. When usage data is visible to thousands of peers, ranks engineers against each other, and lands in a manager's view at calibration time, the line between "investment tracking" and "performance signal" is largely a disclosure choice.

The wider point: this is what production-scale bank engineering looks like in 2026. Lloyds is treating agentic AI as a balance-sheet line; JPMorgan is treating tool adoption as an org-wide measurable. Both bets sit at the centre of the bank, not its periphery. Both rely on the assumption that more usage equals more value — which is precisely where Writer's 23% number, and the productivity research we look at next, deserve a hard read.

The thread for Section 4: the regulator now has its own opinion. On April 1, the BoE and PRA put it in writing.

Source 3 — The Regulator's View

The Bank of England names AI a 2026 supervisory priority

What the April 1 letter actually says

On April 1, Sarah Breeden (Deputy Governor for Financial Stability at the Bank of England) and Sam Woods (Deputy Governor and CEO of the PRA) wrote jointly to the Chancellor and the Secretaries of State for Science, Innovation and Technology, and for Business and Trade. The letter responded to a January 28 request for the regulators to set out how they will enable safe AI innovation. Three commitments stand out: AI is now an explicit PRA supervisory priority for 2026; the BoE will run the fourth biennial sector survey of AI adoption; and the AI Consortium will publish work on agentic AI specifically. Supervisory dialogue with firms will increasingly cover AI risk and governance directly.

The framing is deliberately conservative. As A&O Shearman summarised: existing rules apply to AI, and firms should be ready to demonstrate compliance under those rules. There is no new AI-specific rulebook and no BoE/PRA AI sandbox. The PRA's 2023 Model Risk Management Principles for banks (SS1/23), although technology-agnostic, are now the de facto reference for AI model oversight.

Why the timing matters: DORA enforcement begins this week

The April 1 letter lands in the same month as the second Register of Information submission cycle for DORA. April 30 is the consolidated EU deadline for ROI submissions to the European Supervisory Authorities. As of 2026, regulators have moved from paperwork tolerance to what AQMetrics calls "interventionist supervision" — real-time, data-driven evidence of resilience, not policy documentation. Penalties for financial entities can reach 2% of total annual worldwide turnover. Senior management can face personal fines up to €1m and temporary management bans.

The regulatory pattern is consistent: technology-agnostic frameworks are being read onto agentic AI deployments. The PRA's Model Risk Management Principles will increasingly carry weight for any agent making customer-impacting decisions. The DORA ICT third-party register has to capture the AI providers banks have just signed onboarding contracts with. The DRCF (Digital Regulation Cooperation Forum) has separately published a paper on agentic AI. None of these are "new AI rules." All of them constrain how a bank can scale agents safely.

The scale to internalise

Tyk's January research found that roughly 70% of banks are deploying some form of agentic AI in production. AI Magicx's reading of finance-team adoption shows a 600% year-on-year increase from 7% to 44% in twelve months. Tyk also notes that nearly 50% of banks are creating dedicated roles for AI agent supervision. The supervisory infrastructure is being built bottom-up by individual banks at the same time the regulatory infrastructure is being built top-down by the BoE and PRA. Whether they meet in the middle this year is the question that 2026 will answer.

Case Study

Inside the JPMorgan tracking infrastructure

JPMorgan AI rollout — April 2026

Early April — Anthropic's Claude Code is rolled out to a wider engineer population. Internal dashboard immediately tracks adoption rates and spending across 600-plus seats.

Mid-April — The Global Technology division (~65,000 engineers) is told to demonstrate "meaningful improvement" in code quality and volume by using AI tooling.

April 23 — Business Insider publishes screenshots of the internal scoreboards. Engineers are ranked by Copilot and Claude usage. Dashboards visible to thousands of peers.

April 23 (response) — JPMorgan spokesperson confirms the data is collected to "measure the effectiveness of AI investments" and states it is not used in "performance management."

What the framing reveals

JPMorgan's defence — "this is investment tracking, not performance review" — is technically accurate and operationally tenuous. The same telemetry will inform headcount planning, tool seat allocation, and which teams get scaled access to higher-tier agents. The information asymmetry has been deliberately chosen: every engineer can see where they stand, and so can every manager looking at their org.

For a UK bank engineering leader the case study reads two ways. The first is an operational template: instrumenting AI tool adoption is now table stakes for any bank putting nine figures into agentic AI, because without the telemetry there is no answer to "is this working?" beyond anecdote. The second is a culture warning: when usage is the headline metric, engineers optimise for usage. Whether that translates into the kind of code-quality and review-judgement gains that Writer's 29% are actually capturing is a separate question — and the JPMorgan dashboard, as currently described, does not measure it.

Counterpoint

The productivity research has stopped giving clean answers

If a bank is building dashboards on the premise that more AI usage equals more output, the productivity research deserves a careful read. Three studies released between February and April complicate the picture in different ways.

METR's own caveat: developers most positive about AI dropped out of the study because they wouldn't work without their tools, biasing the data.

+600
Extra characters per month typed by AI users vs. non-users (telemetry on 800 devs)
80%+
Survey respondents reporting productivity increase
JetBrains found behaviour and perception aligned: AI users wrote substantially more code and reported feeling more productive over a 2-year window.

How the data reconciles — and what it doesn't say

The studies aren't contradictory. METR measured time-to-completion on bounded tasks among elite open-source developers; JetBrains measured volume-of-code typed across a broader population using IDE telemetry. They are answering different questions. JetBrains shows AI users produce more code, faster, over time. METR's own commentary is that the 2026 cohort is likely faster than the 2025 cohort, but selection effects make the headline number unreliable.

What none of this tells us — the gap that matters most for an engineering leader — is whether the additional code is the right code. Spotify's published account, captured in LeadDev's January annual survey, was that with 90% of developers using AI daily the company saw a 30% jump in code changes per developer, followed by a spike in code review time, quality concerns, and pipeline strain that leadership did not anticipate. Waydev's reading of CircleCI's 2026 State of Software Delivery report adds the systems-level version: industry-average main branch success rate is 70.8% (against a 90% benchmark), with mean time to recovery at 59.2 minutes.

The point Allstacks's Jeff Keyes made bluntly on April 28: "Move fast, review later" has always been a lie; with AI, it is now an expensive lie. Throughput goes up. Whether durable value goes up depends on whether the validation infrastructure scales with the throughput. That is precisely Writer's 23% finding said in engineering language.

Lessons & Implications

What this changes for a UK banking engineering leader

The Verdict

The agentic AI deployment race in banking is real, the regulatory backstop is forming, and the productivity case is more contested than the headline numbers admit. The job for the next two quarters is not faster adoption — it is building the validation, governance, and review infrastructure that lets the spend compound rather than evaporate.

Throughput is not the metric. Validation is.

If usage dashboards are running, they should be paired with main-branch success rate, MTTR, and review-cycle time on AI-touched commits. CircleCI's 90% main-branch success benchmark and 60-minute MTTR target are reasonable references. Branch-level throughput should be tracked separately from main-branch throughput, because a rising feature-branch number with a flat main-branch number is an integration problem, not a development problem.

Governance is moving from paperwork to evidence.

DORA's April 30 ROI deadline, the BoE/PRA's April 1 letter, and the PRA's 2023 Model Risk Management Principles all point in the same direction: regulators want demonstrable, real-time control over what AI systems are doing in production. The work to do now is mapping every agent in production to (a) the third-party register, (b) the model risk inventory, and (c) the human-in-the-loop accountability chain. The 67% figure on shadow-AI breach exposure makes the unsanctioned tooling sweep urgent rather than optional.

Hiring and review patterns are shifting underneath us.

Allstacks's "hire for editing, not just coding" framing is the cleanest version of the people-pattern shift. The mid-career review-and-edit muscle that used to be earned at senior level is now the entry-level bar. For a hiring rubric, that means testing for spec-writing, review judgement, and the willingness to push back on an agent's output. For an existing team, it means giving people time to review — not booking the time AI saved as net delivery.

Key Takeaways
  • The 23% ROI figure is the planning baseline. Assume most of the spend won't compound unless the operating model changes alongside it.
  • Track main-branch success rate and MTTR on AI-touched commits, not just tool usage. Throughput dashboards alone hide the integration cost.
  • The DORA ROI deadline and the PRA's AI supervisory priority both anchor on the same question: can you show real-time evidence of control?
  • Shadow AI is now a CISO-level number. Two-thirds of executives think it has already cost them — the unsanctioned tool sweep is overdue.
  • Review capacity is the bottleneck most teams haven't budgeted for. AI generates more code; the cost is review time, not seat licences.
