Foreword

Something clarifying happened over the past month. AI adoption crossed from widespread to near-universal — 95% weekly usage in the Pragmatic Engineer survey, 92.6% monthly in DX's 121,000-developer dataset — but the infrastructure around it didn't keep pace. CircleCI's analysis of 28 million workflows shows the consequences: throughput up 59%, main branch delivery down 7%, success rates at a five-year low. Engineers are writing more code than ever. They're shipping it more slowly. That gap is the defining challenge for engineering leadership right now.

Meanwhile, the competitive landscape in UK banking shifted permanently. Revolut secured full PRA authorisation, giving an engineering-led digital platform the same regulatory standing as HSBC. Open banking stopped being a fintech buzzword and became a checkout option on Amazon and eBay. For engineering teams building financial products, the ground has moved under them.

This is the inaugural issue of Engineering Digest — a monthly retrospective for people who lead engineering teams and need signal, not noise. No sponsored content, no hype cycles, no listicles. Just the things that matter, framed through the lens of the people who have to make decisions about them.

The AI Coding Arms Race Enters a New Phase

Two surveys published in the past month give us the clearest picture yet of AI adoption in engineering — and they tell the same story from different angles. The Pragmatic Engineer surveyed 906 engineers and engineering leaders (median 11–15 years experience): 95% use AI tools at least weekly, 75% for half or more of their work, 56% for 70% or more. Laura Tacho's DX study — 121,000 developers across 450+ companies — found 92.6% monthly usage, with 26.9% of production code now AI-authored, up from 22%. This is saturation, not adoption.

Claude Code's eight-month ascent

The Pragmatic Engineer survey's most striking result: Claude Code is now the #1 most-used AI coding tool, overtaking GitHub Copilot and Cursor in just eight months since its May 2025 launch. It commands 46% of "most loved" mentions — 57% when you include Claude models generally. At small companies, where engineers choose their own tools, Claude Code leads at 75%. At enterprises with 10,000+ employees, Copilot dominates at 56%, driven by Microsoft procurement bundling. The market is bifurcated between what engineers choose and what procurement buys.

The trust paradox

Here's the number that should give everyone pause: only 29% of developers trust AI-generated code, down from 40% on Stack Overflow's earlier confidence metric. Yet adoption keeps accelerating. Engineers are using tools they don't trust for the majority of their work. Sonar's survey reinforces the pattern — 72% of developers who've tried AI tools use them daily, and 42% of code is now AI-generated or assisted. The METR study adds another wrinkle: their early 2025 research found AI actually caused a 20% productivity slowdown, but their latest update admits they can't measure reliably anymore because developers refuse to work without AI. When you can't run the control group, the experiment is over.

Staff+ engineers are the heaviest agent users at 63.5%, and agent users are twice as excited about AI as non-users. The enthusiasm gap between power users and everyone else is widening — even as overall trust in AI-generated code declines.

— Data from The Pragmatic Engineer AI Tooling Survey 2026

OpenAI's Codex counter-move

OpenAI isn't conceding the agentic market. Codex tripled to 1.6 million weekly active users following the GPT-5.3 Codex launch in early February, with over a million desktop app downloads and 5x token usage growth. Enterprise customers include Cisco, Nvidia, Ramp, and Rakuten. OpenAI is positioning Codex as "the standard agent" — expanding beyond coding into broader enterprise domains. The contest is no longer about code completion. It's about who owns the agent layer between the engineer and the codebase.

The productivity plateau nobody wants to discuss

DX's data reveals something the adoption headlines obscure: measurable productivity gains have plateaued at roughly 10%. That's real, but it's not the transformation the market is pricing in. Near-universal adoption, declining trust, and plateauing measurable productivity — the gap between the hype narrative and the observable data is widening. For engineering leaders, the practical question isn't "should we use AI tools" (that's settled) but "are we measuring the right things to know whether they're actually helping?"

Sources: The Pragmatic Engineer, Fortune, Sonar, Stack Overflow

After a four-year application process and 18 months in mobilisation, the PRA lifted restrictions on March 11 and Revolut Bank UK Ltd became a fully licensed bank. Eligible deposits are now FSCS-protected up to £120,000 — the same safety net customers get at Barclays, HSBC, or Lloyds. This is the moment the fintech narrative shifts from "digital challengers nibbling at margins" to "a direct competitor with regulatory parity."

The licence unlocks what Revolut has been waiting for: lending and credit products. These are the real revenue engines of banking, and they've been locked behind full authorisation. Current accounts are rolling out gradually to new customers; existing customers remain on the e-money entity for now, with migration over several months. Revolut has committed £3 billion to UK investment and plans to create 1,000 high-skilled jobs.

The competitive reality

Bain & Co's latest analysis projects challenger banks will control 35% of the UK market by 2030, up from roughly 20% today. Chris Skinner's data adds nuance: over 62% of UK customers already use at least one challenger bank, but only 14% have fully abandoned traditional institutions. The battleground isn't customer acquisition — Revolut already has 13 million UK customers. It's becoming the primary banking relationship. FSCS protection and credit products are the tools that make that possible.

The IPO question

Revolut is reportedly exploring a dual London/New York IPO at its $75 billion valuation, which would make it one of the largest fintech listings in history. The Bank of England governor reportedly blocked a chancellor-arranged meeting between Revolut and regulators over concerns about political interference in the licensing process — a signal of how seriously the establishment takes this, and how politically charged fintech regulation has become in the UK.

For anyone building payment infrastructure, loyalty programmes, or customer-facing banking technology: this is a competitor with 13 million customers that just gained the full regulatory platform to offer lending, credit, and protected banking services — powered entirely by an engineering-led operating model that traditional banks cannot replicate without rebuilding from the ground up.

Teams at AI speed

CircleCI's 2026 State of Software Delivery report — 28 million CI/CD workflows analysed across thousands of teams — reveals the paradox at the heart of the AI coding boom. Average throughput jumped 59% year-over-year, the biggest increase since they started tracking. But look at the main branch, where code actually ships, and the picture inverts: delivery throughput for the median team is down 7%. Teams are producing more code than ever and deploying it more slowly.

The numbers get worse the deeper you look. Main branch success rates have fallen to 70.8% — the lowest in five-plus years, against a benchmark of 90%. Recovery times are up 13% to 72 minutes on average. Feature branch throughput for the median team is up 15%, which means more work-in-progress accumulating in branches that aren't making it to production. The pipeline from "code written" to "code shipped" has a bottleneck, and AI is making it wider.

The distribution is the story

The top 5% of teams saw throughput jump 97%. The median team got 4%. The bottom quartile got nothing. AI is amplifying existing delivery strengths, not distributing them. The teams that already had strong CI/CD pipelines, good testing practices, and reliable deployment processes captured nearly all the benefit. Everyone else just generated more code that piles up in branches and fails in pipelines. Mid-sized companies (21–50 employees) are stuck in what CircleCI calls the "messy middle" — lowest throughput, recovery times averaging three hours.

Writing code is no longer the constraint. Review, validation, and integration is where AI-generated code piles up. Fewer than 1 in 20 teams have figured out how to ship at AI speed. The other 19 are drowning in work-in-progress.

— CircleCI 2026 State of Software Delivery

What this means for engineering leaders

AI tooling has solved the code generation problem. It has not solved — and is actively worsening — the code delivery problem. At a 70% success rate, a team running 500 daily changes loses the equivalent of 12 full-time engineers to recovery alone. The teams that will pull ahead are the ones investing as heavily in CI/CD reliability, automated testing, security scanning, and review processes as they are in AI coding tools. Right now, that's fewer than 1 in 20.

Sources: CircleCI, Thoughtworks

Open Banking Hits the Mainstream: Pay by Bank Arrives on Amazon and eBay

Open banking stopped being a fintech conference talking point and became a checkout option for millions of shoppers. Both Amazon and eBay have launched Pay by Bank in the UK — the first major e-commerce platforms to offer account-to-account payments powered by open banking infrastructure. eBay partnered with TrueLayer to handle the integration. When a customer selects Pay by Bank, they authenticate via their banking app's biometrics, authorise the payment, and the money moves over the Faster Payments network. No card details entered. No 1–3 day settlement wait. Instant.

How it works, and why engineers should care

The technical flow is straightforward but the implications are significant. The payment bypasses the card networks entirely — no Visa, no Mastercard, no interchange fees. For merchants, that's a material cost reduction on every transaction. For consumers, biometric authentication via their existing banking app is arguably more secure than typing card numbers into a form. For engineering teams building payment flows: this is a new rail you'll need to support, with different settlement characteristics, different error handling, and different regulatory requirements than card payments.

The adoption context

Open Banking UK describes 2026 as a "ground-breaking year." The Amazon and eBay launches add mainstream visibility to an ecosystem that has been growing quietly — but the Payments Association adds a measured note: trust, commercial viability, and regulation will determine whether open banking achieves mainstream scale or remains an alternative payment method. The challenger bank angle is instructive: British Business Bank data shows challenger banks hold 60% of SME lending, but that figure is unchanged from the prior year — the first time in a decade the share hasn't grown. Growth isn't automatic, even with regulatory tailwinds.

The real question for engineering teams isn't whether to support Pay by Bank — Amazon and eBay have made that decision for the market. It's whether your payment infrastructure is built to handle a world where card payments are one rail among several, not the default.

Sources: Open Banking UK, TrueLayer, Payments Association, British Business Bank

The Leadership Read

Agentic Engineering in Practice: What the Pragmatic Summit Told Us

Simon Willison — prolific open-source contributor, creator of Datasette, and one of the most respected voices in AI-assisted development — sat down for a fireside chat at Gergely Orosz's Pragmatic Summit in San Francisco. The recording was published on March 14. It's the most useful thing published this month for anyone managing an engineering team that uses AI tools.

The adoption curve is real — and your people are at different points on it

Willison described distinct phases engineers move through: starting with ChatGPT for occasional questions, progressing to AI agents that write bits of code, then reaching a tipping point where agents produce more code than the human. The newest phase — one Willison says he's now in — is where "you don't read the code." The agent writes it, runs it, tests it, and you evaluate the output rather than reviewing the implementation. Understanding where each person on your team sits on this curve matters, because they need fundamentally different support at each stage.

Mental exhaustion is the new bottleneck — not laziness

After two hours of that I'm done for the day. I'm mentally exhausted. People worry about skill atrophy and being lazy. I think this is the opposite of that. You have to operate firing on all cylinders if you're going to keep your trio or quadruple of agents busy solving all these different problems.

— Simon Willison, Pragmatic Summit, 2026

Running 3–4 AI agent sessions in parallel is technically possible. Willison does it routinely. But it's cognitively draining in a way traditional coding isn't — the context-switching, quality evaluation, and prompt refinement all happening simultaneously. Two hours of high-intensity agent orchestration can be more productive than a full day of traditional development. The engineers using AI most effectively may look like they're working less. Traditional productivity metrics — hours logged, lines of code, PRs merged — are becoming meaningless as signals of output quality.

Speed is up, but capacity has a ceiling

AI makes individual engineers dramatically faster. But one engineer still cannot scale to a thousand projects. Context-switching between agent sessions is the real bottleneck — not the agent's throughput, but the human's ability to maintain context. This connects directly to the CircleCI data elsewhere in this issue: throughput is up 59%, but only 5% of teams are shipping at AI speed. The constraint has moved from code generation to everything that happens after code is generated.

Maintain a "retry list"

The most practical suggestion: keep track of tasks that AI tools failed at, and re-test them every six months as models improve. Willison describes regularly being surprised when something impossible three months ago now works perfectly. The capabilities of frontier models change faster than anyone's intuitions about them — and the teams that systematically re-test their assumptions will find capabilities that others miss.

What this means for engineering management

Productivity measurement needs rethinking — output is disconnecting from hours. Team practices need to account for the mental intensity of AI-assisted work and build in sustainable rhythms. Investment in tooling access and model selection is becoming as important as investment in headcount. The engineers who lean furthest into AI agents aren't the ones who need less management attention — they're navigating the steepest learning curve, making the most consequential decisions about when to trust and when to verify, and operating at the highest cognitive load.

Outside In

Monzo's Backup Bank: Engineering Resilience When the Cloud Goes Down

In January 2026, Monzo experienced an outage. What happened next was remarkable: instead of customers losing access for two hours, Monzo activated "Stand-in" — a fully independent backup bank that kept card payments, cash withdrawals, and transfers running throughout the incident. Customers barely noticed.

What Stand-in actually is

Monzo Stand-in isn't a degraded mode or a cached read replica. It's a fully independent system capable of processing core banking transactions — card payments, ATM withdrawals, incoming and outgoing transfers — without the primary platform being available. It runs on two independent infrastructure stacks, designed as the "backup of last resort." Monzo first described the architecture in a 2025 blog post titled "Tolerating Full Cloud Outages" and has been building toward it since.

Battle-tested, twice

The January 2026 incident was the second real-world activation. The first was a shorter, one-hour outage in 2024. Both times, Stand-in handled live customer transactions without data loss or service degradation. For a UK bank serving millions of customers, this is an extraordinary engineering achievement — and it directly addresses the operational resilience requirements that regulators have been tightening under frameworks like DORA (the Digital Operational Resilience Act).

Why this matters beyond Monzo

Most organisations' disaster recovery plans are theoretical. They exist in runbooks and are tested in controlled environments with synthetic traffic. Monzo's approach is different: they built a system that has now been validated under real production conditions, with real customer money, twice. The engineering investment is significant — maintaining two independent infrastructure stacks is expensive and operationally complex. But the alternative is downtime during which customers can't access their money, and in regulated banking, that's an existential risk.

For engineering leaders in financial services — or any domain where downtime has regulatory or reputational consequences — the question Monzo has answered is whether it's possible to build genuinely independent failover for a modern cloud-native banking platform. It is. The question for everyone else is whether the investment is worth it before the outage forces the decision.
