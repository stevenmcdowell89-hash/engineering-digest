When the Bombs Hit the Cloud: The AWS Middle East Strikes

On March 1, Iranian drone strikes hit two AWS data centres in the UAE and damaged a third in Bahrain. Two of three availability zones in the UAE went offline — not degraded, offline. 109+ AWS services became unavailable. The physical damage was severe: structural, power, fire, and water damage from suppression systems. AWS initially described "objects" causing a "localized power issue," then confirmed the strikes 36 hours later.

The banking impact was immediate. ADCB mobile banking went down. Emirates NBD was disrupted. Payment platforms Alaan and Hubpay stopped processing. Careem — the region's dominant ride-hailing app — was disrupted. The effects cascaded beyond the Middle East: US-EAST-1 experienced degraded performance, Snowflake reported service issues, and Claude AI saw availability problems. As of mid-March, more than 16 days after the strikes, there was no full restoration date. AWS's recommendation to customers was extraordinary: migrate your workloads to alternate regions.

The data residency paradox

UAE and Bahrain Central Bank mandates require financial data to stay in-region. The region's infrastructure was physically destroyed. Failing over to another region resolves the outage but creates a legal problem — you can't comply with data residency requirements when the region doesn't exist in a functional sense. The Dubai Financial Services Authority issued emergency guidance for temporary cross-region processing, a regulatory improvisation nobody had planned for.

Multi-AZ redundancy within a single region — the standard resilience playbook — assumed that availability zones fail independently through technical faults, not simultaneously through military action. That assumption is broken. For any organisation running production workloads in a geopolitically contested region, the conversation has shifted from "how many AZs?" to "how many regions?" — and what happens to your compliance posture when you can't use the one the regulator requires.

Context: What is multi-AZ?

AWS regions contain multiple Availability Zones (AZs) — physically separate data centres within the same geographic area. Multi-AZ deployment spreads workloads across 2–3 AZs so that if one fails, the others keep running. The assumption: AZs fail independently through hardware faults, power issues, or network problems. That assumption was designed for technical failures, not military strikes that damage multiple facilities simultaneously.

Cascading impact

The strikes didn't stay contained to the Middle East. US-EAST-1 experienced degraded performance. Snowflake reported service issues. Claude AI saw availability problems. $300 billion in Gulf AI and data centre investments are now at risk.

Key Takeaways

  • Multi-AZ ≠ multi-region. Simultaneous AZ failure from physical attack was not in most DR plans.
  • Data residency mandates and disaster recovery are now in direct conflict in contested regions.
  • AWS's advice to migrate workloads is unprecedented — and signals long-term uncertainty for the ME region.
  • Cascading failures to US-EAST-1, Snowflake, and Claude AI show interconnection risks beyond the strike zone.

Developer Experience & Productivity

The AI Delivery Bottleneck

70.8%
Main branch success rate — lowest in five years (benchmark: 90%)

CircleCI's 2026 State of Software Delivery report — 28 million CI/CD workflows analysed — reveals the paradox at the heart of the AI coding boom. Average throughput jumped 59% year-over-year, the biggest increase ever. But on the main branch, where code actually ships, delivery throughput for the median team fell 7%.

Recovery times are up 13% to 72 minutes. Feature branch throughput is up 15% for the median team, meaning more work-in-progress piling up in branches that don't make it to production.

The distribution is stark. The top 5% of teams saw throughput jump 97%. The median got 4%. The bottom quartile got nothing. AI is amplifying existing delivery strengths, not distributing them evenly. Writing code is no longer the constraint — review, validation, and integration is where it all piles up.

Sources: CircleCI, CircleCI Report

After a four-year application process and 18 months in mobilisation, the PRA lifted restrictions on March 11 and Revolut Bank UK Ltd became a fully licensed bank. Eligible deposits are now FSCS-protected up to £120,000 — the same safety net customers get at Barclays or HSBC. This is the moment the fintech narrative shifts from "challenger nibbling at margins" to "direct competitor with regulatory parity."

The licence unlocks lending and credit products — the real revenue engines of banking, locked behind full authorisation until now. Current accounts are rolling out gradually to new customers; existing customers remain on the e-money entity, with migration over months. Revolut has committed £3 billion to UK investment and 1,000 high-skilled jobs. They're also exploring a dual London/New York IPO at their $75 billion valuation. The Bank of England governor reportedly blocked a chancellor-arranged meeting over political interference concerns — a signal of how seriously the establishment takes this.

For anyone building payment infrastructure or customer-facing banking technology in the UK: this is a competitor with 13 million customers that just gained the full platform to offer lending, credit, and protected deposits — powered by an engineering-led operating model traditional banks cannot replicate without rebuilding from scratch.

Sources: Reuters, CNBC, Bloomberg

The Leadership Read

Agentic Engineering in Practice: What the Pragmatic Summit Told Us

Simon Willison — creator of Datasette and one of the most respected voices in AI-assisted development — sat down for a fireside chat at Gergely Orosz's Pragmatic Summit in San Francisco. Published March 14. It's the most useful thing released this month for anyone managing an engineering team that uses AI tools.

The adoption curve is real — and your people are at different points on it

Willison described distinct phases: ChatGPT for occasional questions, then AI agents writing bits of code, then agents producing more code than the human. The newest phase — where Willison operates now — is where "you don't read the code." The agent writes it, runs it, tests it, and you evaluate the output. Understanding where each person on your team sits on this curve matters, because they need fundamentally different support at each stage.

TDD becomes essential, not optional

"Every single coding session I start with an agent, I start by saying here's how to run the test." When AI writes the code, tests become the specification language — the mechanism through which you verify the agent did what you intended. Teams without strong test coverage are flying blind with agents.

Mental exhaustion is the new bottleneck

After two hours of that I'm done for the day. I'm mentally exhausted. People worry about skill atrophy and being lazy. I think this is the opposite of that. You have to operate firing on all cylinders if you're going to keep your trio or quadruple of agents busy solving all these different problems.

— Simon Willison, Pragmatic Summit, 2026

Running 3–4 agent sessions in parallel is technically possible but cognitively draining. Two hours of high-intensity agent orchestration can outproduce a full day of traditional development. The engineers using AI most effectively may look like they're working less. Traditional productivity metrics are becoming meaningless.

Maintain a "retry list"

Keep track of tasks that AI failed at and re-test every six months as models improve. Willison describes regularly being surprised when something impossible three months ago now works perfectly. The teams that systematically re-test their assumptions will find capabilities others miss.

What this means for management

Productivity measurement needs rethinking. Team practices must account for cognitive intensity. Tooling investment is becoming as important as headcount. The engineers leaning furthest into agents aren't the ones who need less attention — they're navigating the steepest learning curve and operating at the highest cognitive load.
