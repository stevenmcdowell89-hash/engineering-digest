Adoption Is Universal — Growth Is Not

JetBrains published its latest AI Pulse survey data on April 2, drawing on responses from more than 10,000 developers across eight languages. The headline number: 74% of developers worldwide have adopted specialised AI coding tools — editors, assistants, and agents — as of January 2026. A further 90% use at least one AI tool (including chatbots) for development work.

But adoption figures mask a dramatic shift in market share. GitHub Copilot remains the most widely recognised tool (76% awareness) and holds the largest single share at 29% workplace usage. That number has barely moved in a year. Copilot's strongest position is in enterprises over 5,000 employees, where it reaches 40% — driven overwhelmingly by procurement defaults, not developer choice.

Claude Code's Eight-Month Ascent

Claude Code, which didn't exist before mid-2025, has reached 18% workplace adoption — matching Cursor for joint second place globally and hitting 24% in the US and Canada. Its customer satisfaction score is the highest in the survey: 91% CSAT and a net promoter score of +54. The Pragmatic Engineer's parallel survey of 900+ engineers tells a similar story: Claude Code is the most-used tool, with directors and VPs among the most enthusiastic adopters.

"GitHub Copilot growth has stalled since last year. Claude Code adoption increased 6x from April–June 2025 to January 2026."

— JetBrains AI Pulse Survey, April 2, 2026

The Kimi Question

Then there's Cursor's Composer 2. Launched in late March, it was marketed as "frontier-level at coding" at a fraction of the cost: $0.50 per million input tokens versus $5 for Claude Opus 4.6. What Cursor didn't disclose at launch was that Composer 2 was built on Kimi K2.5, an open-source model from Chinese startup Moonshot AI. Cofounder Aman Sanger later acknowledged on X that "it was a miss to not mention the Kimi base in our blog from the start." Only about a quarter of the compute came from the base model, with Cursor's own reinforcement learning training on top — but the provenance disclosure gap raises a procurement question that many enterprises haven't yet been forced to answer.

Three-Way Tension

Engineering directors making tooling decisions in 2026 face a tension that didn't exist eighteen months ago. Developers increasingly prefer Claude Code. Procurement teams default to Copilot because it's the known quantity with a Microsoft contract attached. And the market's cost frontier is being pushed by models built on open-source Chinese foundations — capable, cheap, and sitting outside most enterprise AI governance policies. The Kimi episode is the first visible collision between these three forces. It will not be the last.

The takeaway: The AI coding tools market has moved from "should we adopt?" to a three-way contest between developer preference, procurement defaults, and model provenance — and most enterprise tooling policies haven't caught up with any of them.

Wise Takes On High Street Banks

Wise launched its UK Current Account on March 30, offering a 3.26% variable rate on GBP balances — a direct challenge to the high street banks sitting on over £250bn in zero-interest current accounts. The numbers behind the move are substantial: more than 3 million active UK customers, £8bn held in Wise accounts, and over £4bn already earning interest through Wise Assets.

The current account comes packaged with Airport Lounge Pass access and Young Explorer cards for under-18s, both launched after sustained customer demand. Wise's real-time payment infrastructure already delivers 74% of global payments in under 20 seconds — a capability that most traditional banks are still years away from matching at scale.

The takeaway: Wise has built the multi-currency real-time infrastructure that traditional banks are spending years trying to modernise, and is now competing directly on the most defended product in retail banking — the current account.

Node.js 20 — 27 Days to End of Life

Node.js 20 reaches end-of-life on April 30 — 27 days from today. After that date, no more official security patches or vulnerability fixes. AWS Lambda Phase 1 deprecation begins the same day, blocking the creation of new Lambda functions on the nodejs20.x runtime. Phase 2 follows in August; Phase 3 in September.

The recommended migration target is Node.js 24, not 22 — Node.js 22's own EOL arrives in April 2027, uncomfortably close. But Node.js 24 introduces a breaking change: callback-based Lambda handlers (exports.handler = function(event, context, callback)) no longer work. Every Lambda function using the legacy callback pattern needs rewriting to async/await.

Separately, Node.js announced it is moving to one major release per year starting with Node 27 in April 2027. Every release becomes LTS. The confusing odd/even system is retired.

The takeaway: Any team running production workloads on Node.js 20 without a migration plan has a 27-day security deadline — and the migration path to Node.js 24 includes a breaking change that affects every Lambda function using the legacy callback pattern.

The Leadership Read

The Tool Your Developers Want vs. The Tool Procurement Approves

The data is in — and it tells two different stories

JetBrains' AI Pulse survey, published April 2, gives engineering leaders the clearest picture yet of what's actually happening with AI coding tools. The headline — 74% adoption — sounds like consensus. Beneath it, there's a widening gap between what developers choose and what enterprises buy.

GitHub Copilot holds 76% awareness and 29% workplace usage, but that number has flatlined. At companies over 5,000 employees, Copilot reaches 40%, largely because procurement teams select it as the default via existing Microsoft agreements. Meanwhile, Claude Code — barely eight months old — has hit 18% globally and 24% in the US and Canada, with a 91% customer satisfaction score and a net promoter score of +54, the highest in the survey.

The agent adoption gap

The Pragmatic Engineer's parallel survey of 900+ engineers adds a layer the JetBrains data doesn't fully capture. Staff+ engineers are the heaviest users of AI agents, at 63.5%. Directors and VPs are especially enthusiastic about agentic tools. Most engineers juggle two to four AI tools simultaneously; 15% use five or more. The era of a single company-approved tool is over in practice, even where it persists in policy.

"GitHub Copilot growth has stalled since last year. Claude Code increased adoption 6x from April–June 2025 to January 2026, achieving the highest satisfaction score of any tool in the survey."

— JetBrains AI Pulse Survey, April 2, 2026

Cursor's 35% growth advantage

Cursor is growing 35% faster than Copilot per the Pragmatic Engineer data, driven by developers who prize speed and cost-effectiveness over procurement convenience. And Cursor's trajectory raises the sharpest question of all: its newest model is built on a Chinese open-source foundation that most enterprise AI governance policies don't yet address.

The pattern across both surveys is consistent: developers are adopting tools faster than procurement can evaluate them, and the tools developers prefer are frequently not the ones procurement approves.

Outside In

Clerk's Reliability Reckoning

When routine infrastructure fails silently

Clerk, the authentication-as-a-service provider used by thousands of applications, published a post-mortem on March 13 for a service outage three days earlier. The root cause was a failed live migration of a Google Cloud SQL virtual machine — a routine operation performed by Google without advance notice. The migration caused a spike in disk latency, which cascaded into database lock contention and a complete outage.

This was not Clerk's first recent incident. A similar Cloud SQL live migration had caused problems in September 2025. The database was pinned to prevent further migrations until January 2026, then unpinned. Three months later, the same failure mode struck again.

"Clerk has faced several incidents recently and frustration is rightfully mounting. We have failed at our commitment to customers and we are deeply sorry. Customers depend on Clerk for critical infrastructure, and we have not lived up to that responsibility."

Masking failure state

A technical detail in the post-mortem is worth noting: during the outage, Clerk's system returned 429 (rate limit) status codes instead of 500 (server error) codes. The 429s bubbled up from an internal service rather than being transformed to accurately represent the failure. Applications relying on those error codes to assess Clerk's health were getting a misleading signal — the system looked throttled when it was actually down.

The leadership decision

CEO Colin Sidoti announced that the majority of Clerk's engineering team would shift to reliability-focused projects, with new product features explicitly delayed as a consequence. For an infrastructure provider in a competitive market, pausing feature development is a significant strategic cost — and an acknowledgement that reliability debt had outpaced the roadmap.

The takeaway: When an infrastructure provider's routine operation fails silently and takes your platform down, the response that matters is whether you're willing to redirect your entire engineering team to reliability and tell customers that features will be late.
