Banking & fintech

The UK government just told financial services teams to build for agentic payments — now

FCA AI Live Testing Lab — first cohort

Barclays · GoCardless · Experian · UBS · Palindrome · Aereve · Co-Adjute · Lloyds/Scottish Widows

Supervised agentic deployment under FCA oversight. Next Supercharged Sandbox intake: May 5, 2026.

On April 21, during UK Fintech Week, HM Treasury and the FCA published a package that removes much of the "wait for clarity" reason teams have been holding back on agentic payment infrastructure. The announcement combined four distinct moves into a single signal: Open Banking comes under direct FCA regulation; the PSR merges into the FCA; a Wholesale Digital Markets Champion is appointed; and eight companies begin supervised AI deployment in financial services.

What the FCA is actually doing with Open Banking

The transfer of Open Banking oversight from JROC and OBIE to direct FCA regulation is the structural change that carries the most near-term engineering consequence. A single regulator with enforcement powers over API specifications and SLA regimes creates a more predictable compliance target than a joint committee. Teams building on Open Banking rails now have a single supervisory point of contact — and a single enforcement entity if something breaks.

The PSR merger accelerates this. Payment Systems Regulator remit (Faster Payments, card schemes, interbank infrastructure) combines with FCA remit (conduct, consumer duty, AI oversight) under one body. The likely outcome for engineering teams is a unified compliance framework that spans payment initiation and the consumer-facing layer above it.

Agentic commerce gets explicit regulatory recognition

Jessica Rusu's IFGS keynote framing of an "agentic commerce era" was the phrase that circulated most from the week's announcements. The more consequential move was structural: establishing an AI Live Testing Lab with eight named cohort participants gives regulated firms a supervised pathway for deploying AI agents in payment flows. Barclays and Lloyds/Scottish Widows are inside that pathway. For their engineering counterparts at other institutions, the timeline pressure is real: the firms in the cohort are building operational experience while others deliberate.

"The era of agentic commerce is already here — we just need the infrastructure to catch up."

Jessica Rusu, Chief Data, Intelligence and Information Officer, FCA — IFGS keynote, April 21, 2026 · fca.org.uk

The engineering implications, by layer

Three layers of this package have direct engineering consequence:

API and SLA governance. Open Banking under single-regulator oversight means tighter and more consistent enforcement of API performance requirements. Teams maintaining Open Banking integrations should expect specification rigidity to increase, not decrease, under the new regime.

AI agent compliance surface. The FCA's "no new rules" posture means existing operational resilience obligations — explainability, incident reporting, consumer duty — apply to AI agents in payment flows without modification. That shifts complexity to design-time: teams need to embed compliance into agent architecture before deployment, not as an audit layer after.

Wholesale settlement infrastructure. Chris Woolard's appointment as Wholesale Digital Markets Champion signals formal government backing for tokenised settlement infrastructure. This is a longer-cycle item — but the appointment creates a formal consultation channel that teams building on DLT-adjacent infrastructure should engage with early.

The end of the non-technical engineering manager

James Stanier's LeadDev piece is generating the kind of quiet circulation that means it is landing with people who don't want to say it out loud in a meeting. His argument: as AI tools produce more code, faster, the manager who cannot read or review that output is structurally disadvantaged. They cannot assess quality, spot drift, challenge estimates, or have credible technical conversations with their team.

Stanier is precise about the threshold: technical conversance, meaning the ability to engage with engineering decisions at a level above "trust me." Writing production code is a separate and higher bar. Reading, reviewing, and challenging it is the baseline he describes. AI-generated code volume is raising the stakes on that baseline. When a team can ship more with less friction, the manager's value in maintaining quality and direction becomes more visible, not less.

For engineering organisations that have moved toward deliberately non-technical management layers — often for legitimate reasons around people focus and delivery management — this is worth sitting with. The pressure Stanier describes will not arrive as a policy change. It will arrive as a gradual credibility gap.

The takeaway: AI tooling is raising the technical floor for credible engineering leadership, without any formal role definition changing.

Feature

Node.js 20 dies on April 30. Your cloud provider is already making plans.

Six days from publication, Node.js 20 reaches end-of-life. That sentence would normally belong in On the Radar and Platform Updates — and it does appear there too — but the cloud provider behaviour that follows the EOL date makes this worth a longer read than a table row.

What EOL means in practice

Node.js EOL means no further security patches from the Node.js release team. For teams running Node 20 in containerised environments they control, the practical risk is manageable: you keep running it, you accept the security posture, you upgrade when it suits your cycle. For teams relying on managed cloud runtimes, it is different. AWS Lambda, Azure Functions, and Google Cloud Functions all freeze runtime update support for EOL Node versions shortly after the official date — in some cases, the next day. That freeze means no environment patches, no security backports, and eventually the function update capability itself stops accepting EOL runtime versions.

Down the Rabbit Hole Lara Hogan — "AI aha" team meetings

Lara Hogan's recent writing on how to structure the AI conversation inside an engineering team — moving beyond individual tool adoption to shared team understanding. A natural companion to the Stanier piece on technical conversance this issue.

Long-read

The migration path

Node.js 22 is the current LTS release and the standard migration target. Node.js 24 LTS arrived in October 2025 and is the forward-looking choice for teams willing to take a slightly newer release. Both are actively maintained. The migration from Node 20 to 22 is generally clean — the breaking changes are minor for most application code — but the friction sits in CI pipeline configuration, Dockerfile base images, and any third-party dependencies that pinned to Node 20 behaviours.

Where teams get caught

The common failure pattern: a team hears about Node 20 EOL, plans the migration, executes it across their main services — and leaves a single overlooked function or background worker behind. Cloud provider freeze applies per function configuration. One forgotten Lambda function on Node 20 becomes an unpatched attack surface on a runtime the provider no longer touches.

The Stack

Tokenised Settlement in Wholesale Markets

Chris Woolard's appointment as Wholesale Digital Markets Champion makes this the right week to understand what tokenisation actually means for the teams building financial infrastructure.

Key terms
Tokenisation
Representing a financial instrument — a bond, equity, or payment obligation — as a programmable token on a distributed ledger. The token carries ownership rights and can be transferred, settled, or used as collateral without a central intermediary processing each step.
Atomic DvP (Delivery versus Payment)
Settlement where asset transfer and cash payment happen simultaneously in a single transaction — eliminating the settlement lag (currently T+1 or T+2) and the counterparty risk that exists during that lag.
ISO 20022
The global messaging standard for financial transactions. Tokenised settlement systems are being designed to interoperate with ISO 20022 message flows — which matters for teams already building on SWIFT's migration timeline.

Why it matters for your teams

Tokenised settlement changes three things that engineering teams currently manage as separate systems: asset ledger, payment ledger, and settlement reconciliation. In a tokenised model, these collapse into a single programmable layer. The operational complexity does not disappear — it moves. Reconciliation logic that currently lives in batch jobs and overnight settlement windows shifts to smart contract logic and real-time event streams. The engineering surface area changes shape rather than shrinks.

For banking engineering teams, the near-term consequence is API and data architecture decisions made today that need to accommodate tokenised instrument types tomorrow. If your settlement data models assume T+2 batch flows, they will need rearchitecting for atomic finality. If your risk systems assume counterparty exposure during settlement windows, that assumption breaks in a DvP-atomic world.

What to watch for

Three initiatives are worth tracking. Project Agorá (BIS, seven central banks) is a live pilot testing wholesale FX settlement on a unified ledger — results will establish whether atomic DvP works at system scale. The UK DLT Pilot Regime, operating inside the FCA sandbox, is testing gilt and securities tokenisation with a small number of institutions. The Regulated Liability Network is a UK banking consortium exploring a shared tokenised sterling layer for interbank settlement. Chris Woolard's new remit spans all three, and his formal appointment creates a consultation channel that did not previously exist at this level of seniority.

Leadership read

AI made platform engineering strategic again

The argument

Platform engineering teams spent several years fighting a perception problem: expensive to run, slow to deliver value, hard to justify at board level. A LeadDev piece from April 13 — which has gained significant traction this week — makes a direct case that AI agents have changed the calculus. The author's argument: agents need golden paths, safe sandboxes, and robust observability to operate reliably. Those are exactly what mature platform engineering delivers. The demand signal for platform investment has arrived from an unexpected direction.

What changed, and why it matters

The mechanism is straightforward. When developers use AI code assistants, the output quality is highly dependent on the quality of the environment those assistants are working in — clean APIs, reliable test infrastructure, fast feedback loops. An AI agent operating in a chaotic environment produces chaotic output faster. Platform engineering, by reducing environmental entropy, directly improves the ceiling on what AI-assisted development can deliver.

For platform teams that have struggled to demonstrate ROI in business terms, this reframes the value proposition. The argument for investing in internal developer platforms was always productivity — but AI tooling makes the mechanism more legible to non-engineers: better infrastructure means better AI output means faster delivery.

The risk the piece identifies

Platform teams that are reacting to AI adoption — retrofitting observability and sandboxing after agents are already deployed — are building catch-up debt. The teams that built ahead, treating agentic workloads as first-class platform citizens before they arrived, are starting to see compounding advantage. The gap between these two cohorts is widening, not closing.

"The teams treating AI agents as first-class platform consumers — before they were mainstream — now have infrastructure that works. The teams who waited are catching up under pressure."

LeadDev, April 13, 2026
This could be worth talking about: whether our platform roadmap is investing ahead of AI agent adoption, or reacting after the first agents are already in production.

Suhail Patel at QCon London: shipping constantly with humans and LLMs

Suhail Patel (Principal Engineer, Monzo) presented at QCon London on how Monzo manages continuous deployment across a microservices estate at scale — and what changes when LLMs enter the development loop. The core lesson from the talk: shipping velocity is a cultural and process property, not a tooling property. LLMs accelerate code production; they do not automatically improve the system that decides what to ship and how to validate it.

Patel's QCon talk (available via InfoQ) covers the tension between developer autonomy and system coherence at an organisation that has grown from dozens to 1,600+ engineers without losing its deployment cadence. The practical question Monzo has had to answer repeatedly: how do you maintain fast deployment cycles when the organisation is large enough that no single person understands the full system?

The Android R8 result

Separately from the QCon talk, Monzo's Android team published results from their R8 code-shrinking optimisation work — a 35% reduction in Application Not Responding (ANR) errors. The mechanism: R8 shrinks and optimises bytecode at build time, reducing the runtime overhead that contributes to ANR events. For a bank processing real-money transactions on mobile, ANR rates are a direct reliability metric, and a 35% improvement at scale is a meaningful signal about the compounding return on build tooling investment.

The two data points sit alongside each other instructively: Patel's talk addresses the process discipline required to ship frequently at scale; the Android result illustrates the kind of infrastructure investment that makes mobile reliability systematic rather than ad hoc.

The takeaway: At the scale Monzo operates, deployment velocity and mobile reliability are both outcomes of deliberate platform investment — neither emerges naturally from team size or engineering talent alone.
