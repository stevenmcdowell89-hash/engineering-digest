Banking & Fintech

Starling Launches the UK's First Agentic AI Banking Assistant

Data: Starling Bank

What Starling built

Starling's "Starling Assistant," launched March 20 for personal account holders on an opt-in basis, is a conversational banking interface that responds to voice and natural language prompts. It doesn't just answer questions — it acts. The assistant can set up personalised saving goals, organise bill payments, share spending insights, and provide tailored banking guidance, all executing directly on the customer's account. Built on Google Gemini running within Starling's Google Cloud environment, all customer data stays within that perimeter and is never used for model training.

Eight years of infrastructure, three phases of AI

The launch is the culmination of eight years of AI development at Starling. The progression is worth studying: Starling moved from analytical AI (Spending Intelligence — pattern recognition in spending data, launched 2025) to advisory AI (Scam Intelligence — detecting and alerting on marketplace scams) to agentic AI (taking actions on customer accounts). Each layer built on the infrastructure and trust model of the one before it. The Starling Assistant now surfaces both earlier tools alongside new agentic capabilities through a single conversational interface.

The competitive signal

This matters because it sets a benchmark. Starling is the first UK bank to give an AI write access to customer accounts in a production setting. The opt-in model and the progressive capability build suggest a deliberate approach to regulatory and customer trust. But the competitive clock has started. Revolut posted £1.7B in profit this week and is expanding its UK banking operations with 68.3M customers globally. The FCA's targeted support gateway opens April 6. For engineering teams at UK banks, the question is no longer whether to build agentic capabilities, but how quickly your architecture, trust model, and compliance framework can support them.

What engineering leaders should watch

The hardest problem here is not the AI model — it is the control plane around it. When an assistant has the ability to move money, set up recurring transfers, and modify account configurations, the blast radius of a model error or prompt injection is fundamentally different from a chatbot that just retrieves information. Starling's decision to build incrementally — proving out analytical and advisory layers before granting agentic access — is the engineering pattern worth emulating. The teams that skip the trust-building phases and jump straight to agentic will discover why Starling didn't.

Key Takeaways
  • Starling is the first UK bank to deploy agentic AI that acts on customer accounts — not just answers questions
  • The progressive build from analytical → advisory → agentic AI is the architectural pattern — each layer proved the trust model before the next was built
  • The competitive benchmark is now set: Revolut scaling, FCA rules landing April 6, and agentic AI in production at a UK neobank
  • The control plane challenge — rollback, blast-radius containment, prompt injection defence — is harder when the AI has write access to accounts

XBOW Embeds AI Pen-Testing in Microsoft Security Copilot

XBOW, the autonomous offensive security platform that made history by receiving the first-ever AI CVE attribution (CVE-2026-21536, a critical unauthenticated RCE), has announced a deeper integration with Microsoft. The collaboration embeds continuous AI-driven penetration testing directly into Security Copilot and Sentinel through three components: a Pentest Manager Agent, a Sentinel Connector, and a Pentest Analysis Agent.

Available as a public preview at RSAC 2026, this shifts the model from periodic manual pen-testing engagements to continuous, automated offensive assessment. The practical question for security teams is not whether AI pen-testing works — XBOW's CVE record proves it does — but how vulnerability management workflows handle the order-of-magnitude increase in discovered issues when scanning never stops.

Source: XBOW

GitHub Training Data Opt-In Takes Effect

Two AI Tooling Strategies Diverge

GitHub announced that from April 24, interaction data from Copilot Free, Pro, and Pro+ users will be used to train AI models. Business and Enterprise plans are unaffected. The nuance matters: private repository content at rest is excluded, but interaction data generated while working in private repos — inputs, outputs, code snippets, and associated context — is covered. Opt-out is available in settings.

The same week, Cursor shipped self-hosted cloud agents that keep code and tool execution entirely within your own network. These two announcements illustrate a stark divergence: GitHub is centralising data to improve its models; Cursor is decentralising execution to win trust. For engineering leaders evaluating code assistant governance, the question is no longer just capability — it is where your interaction data lives and who trains on it.

Sources: GitHub Blog, Cursor

The Leadership Read

The Preceptorship Model: Growing Engineers When AI Handles the Easy Work

Microsoft Azure CTO Mark Russinovich and VP of Developer Community Scott Hanselman have published a paper proposing a nursing-style "preceptorship" model for developing junior software engineers. Their argument: as AI automates the tasks that used to build foundational skills — writing boilerplate, debugging simple issues, reading documentation — organisations face a pipeline crisis that takes years to materialise and years to repair.

How it works

Preceptorship pairs early-career developers with senior engineers at 3:1 to 5:1 ratios. The juniors work on real product teams — prompting, debugging, and reviewing AI-generated code — while seniors externalise their judgment, turning daily work into structured learning. AI tools are deliberately configured for Socratic coaching rather than direct code generation: asking "why did you choose this approach?" instead of just producing the answer. Each cohort runs for at least one year.

"The path forward is a culture of preceptorship at scale. We must enable senior mentorship with AI systems that capture reasoning, surface misconceptions, and turn daily work into teachable moments."

— Mark Russinovich and Scott Hanselman, Microsoft

The cautionary data point

The paper references Microsoft's own EDS Systems Engineering Development program, which was paused with a three-month recovery estimate. Actual recovery took over 18 months. Russinovich and Hanselman's point is stark: the talent pipeline is not a headcount lever — it is a supply chain, and supply chains are rebuilt, not restarted. The short-term incentive to stop hiring juniors because AI fills the gap will produce a future without the engineers capable of governing the AI systems being deployed today.

What happened

On March 10, Nebius — a cloud GPU provider relevant to teams running AI workloads — experienced a major outage in its us-central1 region during what was supposed to be a controlled power infrastructure maintenance window. The provider-led electrical work did not remain within the announced maintenance profile. Instead of a single controlled event, it produced an initial multi-rack power outage at 15:18 UTC followed by at least four additional unplanned power interruptions over the next several hours, each disrupting recovery work already in progress.

Why the duration compounded

The site was operating in a temporary power configuration during maintenance. When an electrical anomaly caused incorrect switching and rack ATS unit failures, UPS capacity was insufficient to prevent the cascade. But the real duration driver was architectural: customer-facing networking, VM connectivity, Managed Kubernetes, and storage all depended on shared regional networking and a Kubernetes-based control plane. Power returned to different parts of the site at different times, meaning some hosts recovered before the control services needed to restart their workloads were healthy. The result: many workloads required manual reconciliation rather than automatic recovery.

The honest admission

Nebius acknowledged that during the incident, they did not follow the most effective restoration algorithm — too much recovery was handled as parallel service-by-service restoration instead of a stricter dependency-ordered regional recovery sequence. Their action items include hard stop-and-review criteria for ongoing electrical work and regular recovery drills with dependency-ordered restoration sequences. For any team relying on GPU cloud providers for AI workloads, this post-mortem is worth reading in full.
