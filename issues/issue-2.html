Foreword

Speed without guardrails is just a faster way to break things. The irony is sharp: the same week, Plandek's benchmarks showed AI delivering its biggest gains to the teams least equipped to handle the output. The bottleneck has moved, and it is sitting squarely in review, approval, and integration.

A pattern, not an incident

Amazon SVP Dave Treadwell told staff that a "trend of incidents" had emerged since Q3 2025, culminating in several major outages in the last few weeks. On March 2, Amazon's AI coding assistant Q contributed to a disruption that caused incorrect delivery times across global marketplaces — 1.6 million errors and roughly 120,000 lost orders. Three days later, a far worse outage caused a 99% drop in orders across North American marketplaces, resulting in 6.3 million lost orders. The root cause: a high-blast-radius config change deployed by a single operator with no automated pre-deployment validation.

The control plane gap

The pattern Treadwell identified is one engineering leaders across industries will recognise. AI tools accelerate code generation, but the infrastructure meant to validate, approve, and safely deploy that code has not kept pace. Amazon's internal documents are blunt: "GenAI's usage in control plane operations will accelerate exposure of sharp edges and places where guardrails do not exist." The incidents exposed missing or bypassed two-person authorisation requirements, absent modelled change management processes, and data corruption that took hours to unwind.

Data: Business Insider

Controlled friction as strategy

Amazon's response is a 90-day temporary safety guideline targeting approximately 335 Tier-1 systems — services that directly impact consumers. Engineers must now secure two-person review before any code change, use a formal documentation and approval tool, and run automated validation that adheres to central reliability engineering rules. Treadwell framed the approach as "controlled friction": slowing down deployment velocity in the short term to rebuild trust in the deployment pipeline.

What matters for engineering leaders

The most telling line in Amazon's internal documents is the plan to invest in "both deterministic and agentic safeguards." The company is not retreating from AI — it is building the safety infrastructure that should have scaled alongside AI adoption. This is not an Amazon-specific problem. Any organisation that increased deployment velocity through AI tooling without proportionally investing in review, approval, and blast-radius containment will hit the same wall. The question is whether you find it through an internal audit or through a production incident.

Key Takeaways
  • AI-assisted code generation has outpaced deployment safety infrastructure at scale — Amazon's incidents are the most visible example of a systemic gap
  • Two-person authorisation, modelled change management, and automated pre-deployment validation are not bureaucracy — they are the control plane for AI-accelerated engineering
  • The fix is not to slow AI adoption but to invest in the review and approval systems that contain its blast radius

Mastercard Bridges Fiat and Stablecoin Rails

Mastercard's agreement to acquire London-based stablecoin infrastructure startup BVNK for up to $1.8B (including $300M contingent) is the clearest signal yet that traditional payment networks view digital asset infrastructure as a core capability, not an experiment. BVNK processes $30B in payments annually across 130+ countries and connects to all major blockchain networks.

The engineering implications are concrete: BVNK will power stablecoin capabilities across Mastercard's payment endpoints, enabling 24/7 stablecoin settlement for processors and acquirers, while Mastercard provides global fiat infrastructure including push-to-card, account, and wallet services. For engineering teams at banks and payment providers already on Mastercard rails, this creates a new integration surface that will likely become a product requirement within 18 months.

"BVNK will power stablecoin capabilities across Mastercard's payment endpoints — enabling 24/7 stablecoin settlement for processors and acquirers."

— Jesse Hemson Struthers, CEO, BVNK

Sources: Mastercard, Payments Dive

An AI Agent Found a Critical Windows Vulnerability

XBOW, an autonomous offensive security platform, has been officially credited by Microsoft with discovering CVE-2026-21536 — a critical remote code execution vulnerability in Microsoft Devices Pricing Program. It is the first time a fully autonomous AI agent has received CVE attribution for a critical vulnerability discovery. The flaw allows unauthenticated remote code execution via unrestricted file upload, with no user interaction required.

XBOW has now announced a deeper integration with Microsoft, embedding continuous penetration testing directly into Microsoft Security Copilot and Sentinel. For security teams, this marks a shift from periodic manual pen-testing to continuous, AI-driven offensive assessment. The practical question is not whether AI pen-testing works — it does — but how organisations structure their vulnerability management workflows when the volume of discovered issues increases by an order of magnitude.

The Leadership Read

The Preceptorship Model: A Blueprint for Growing Engineers in the AI Era

Microsoft Azure CTO Mark Russinovich and VP of Developer Community Scott Hanselman have published a paper proposing a nursing-style "preceptorship" model for developing junior software engineers. Their argument is direct: as AI automates the tasks that used to build foundational skills, organisations face a pipeline crisis measured in years, not months.

The core proposal

Preceptorship pairs early-in-career developers with senior engineers at 3:1 to 5:1 ratios. The juniors work on real product teams — prompting, debugging, and reviewing AI-generated code — while seniors externalise their judgment, turning daily work into structured learning. AI tools are configured for Socratic coaching rather than immediate code generation. Each cohort runs at least one year.

"The path forward is a culture of preceptorship at scale. We must enable senior mentorship with AI systems that capture reasoning, surface misconceptions, and turn daily work into teachable moments."

— Mark Russinovich and Scott Hanselman, Microsoft

Why this matters now

The paper references Microsoft's own EDS Systems Engineering Development program, which was paused with a three-month recovery estimate. Actual recovery took over 18 months. Russinovich and Hanselman's point is stark: the talent pipeline is not a headcount lever — it is a supply chain, and supply chains are rebuilt, not restarted. Organisations acting on the short-term incentive to stop hiring juniors will arrive at a future without the engineers capable of governing the AI systems they are deploying today.

What Starling built

Starling's "Starling Assistant," launched March 20 for personal account holders on an opt-in basis, is a conversational banking interface that responds to voice and natural language prompts. It can set up personalised saving goals, organise bill payments, share spending insights, and provide general banking guidance — all within the app, acting on the customer's behalf rather than just answering questions.

The engineering choices

The assistant is built on Google Gemini running on Google Cloud, extending the Spending Intelligence and Scam Intelligence tools Starling shipped last year. The progression is instructive: Starling moved from analytical AI (pattern recognition in spending data) to advisory AI (scam detection alerts) to agentic AI (taking actions on customer accounts). Each layer built on the infrastructure and trust model of the one before it.

Why engineering leaders should pay attention

Starling has positioned itself as the first UK bank to deploy agentic AI in a customer-facing context. The opt-in rollout model and the progression from analytics to agency suggest a deliberate approach to managing both regulatory risk and customer trust. For engineering teams at other UK banks, this sets the competitive benchmark: the question is no longer whether to build agentic banking capabilities, but how quickly your architecture can support them.
