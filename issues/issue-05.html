AI & Software Economics

The SaaSpocalypse: When AI Agents Ate the Software Industry

$2 trillion in SaaS market cap erased. Atlassian cut 1,600 people. And Goldman Sachs says 60% of software economics will flow through agents by 2030. The per-seat model is dying.

In early February, an investor sell-off wiped nearly $1 trillion from software stocks in a single week. By the end of the month, the total was closer to $2 trillion. The IGV Software Index dropped 30% from its September peak. Short sellers profited over $20 billion.

The catalyst wasn't earnings misses or a macro shock. It was a structural reassessment: AI agents don't just help users work inside software — they replace the need for the software entirely. When one agent handles the work of five CRM operators, you don't need five seats. The per-seat model — the engine that powered two decades of SaaS valuations — is breaking.

Then on March 11, Atlassian made it concrete. The company cut 1,600 people — 10% of its workforce — to fund its AI pivot. Over 900 of those were in R&D. The CTO was replaced with two co-CTOs. The stock, already down 53% year-to-date, barely moved. The market had already priced in the restructuring.

CEO Mike Cannon-Brookes was unusually direct. "Our approach is not 'AI replaces people,'" he wrote. "But it would be disingenuous to pretend AI doesn't change the mix of skills we need or the number of roles required in certain areas. It does."

Atlassian is not alone. Salesforce is down 28%. ServiceNow -22%. Workday -20%. HubSpot -25%. The pattern: companies whose core workflows — task tracking, CRM updates, customer support triage, report generation — are exactly what AI agents automate.

Goldman Sachs analysis predicts AI agents will broaden the overall software market but capture disproportionate profit. By 2030, over 60% of software economics may flow through agent-based systems rather than traditional SaaS licences. Software becomes the container. Intelligence becomes the product.

Key Takeaways

  • $2 trillion in SaaS market cap erased in 30 days — not from earnings misses but from structural repricing of the per-seat model.
  • Atlassian's 1,600-person cut is the clearest signal yet: even the companies building developer tools are restructuring around AI.
  • Agent-native startups are competing directly with established vendors at a fraction of the cost — no complex UIs, no onboarding flows, no per-seat billing.
  • Goldman Sachs: 60%+ of software economics through agent-based systems by 2030.

Sources: TechCrunch, Forbes, Reuters, Business Insider, Digital Applied, Bloomberg

AI & Tooling

GPT-5.4, Mistral Small 4, and the Agent-Native Race

67.3%
GPT-5.4 WebArena browser-use success rate

Three model releases in two weeks reshaped the landscape. OpenAI's GPT-5.4 (March 5) is the first general-purpose model with native computer-use capabilities and a 1M-token context window. Its new tool search API lets agents find the right tools without bloated system prompts — a fundamental shift for agentic architectures. Hallucinations dropped 33% versus GPT-5.2. On WebArena-Verified, it scored 67.3% on browser-use tasks — the highest of any foundation model.

Mistral's Small 4 (March 16) went the opposite direction: open-source, Apache 2.0, 119B parameters with only 6.5B active per token. It unifies instruct, reasoning, and coding into a single model with 256K context and configurable reasoning effort. Completion time dropped 40% versus Small 3, with 3x throughput.

Meanwhile, Cerebras signed a deal to deploy its CS-3 wafer-scale systems in AWS data centres (March 13), bringing the industry's fastest inference to AWS Bedrock. The pattern: foundation model providers are optimising for the agentic use case — speed, tool-use, and efficiency — not chat.

Sources: OpenAI, TechCrunch, Mistral docs, Simon Willison, Cerebras / WSJ

Chrome Zero-Days and the NCSC Cyber Warning

⚠ Patch Now

CVE-2026-3909 (Skia) and CVE-2026-3910 (V8) are chainable — memory corruption plus sandbox escape. CISA KEV catalog. Federal deadline: March 27. 3.5 billion Chrome users at risk.

Google patched two high-severity Chrome zero-days on March 13, both already exploited in the wild. CVE-2026-3909 is an out-of-bounds write in Skia, Chrome's 2D graphics library. CVE-2026-3910 is a V8 JavaScript engine flaw allowing arbitrary code execution inside the sandbox. Together, they form a chainable attack surface — Skia for memory corruption, V8 for sandbox escape.

Separately, the UK's National Cyber Security Centre warned organisations to review their security posture following the Middle East conflict. The NCSC assessed "almost certainly a heightened risk of indirect cyber threats" for organisations with operations, partnerships, or supply chains in the region. Pro-Iran hacktivist groups — Handala, Cyber Islamic Resistance, RipperSec — have announced coordinated operations. Palo Alto's Unit 42 noted Iran's internet connectivity dropped to 1-4%, which may limit state-sponsored activity but not proxy operations outside the country.

Sources: The Hacker News, Forbes, Malwarebytes, NCSC, Industrial Cyber

Banking & Fintech

UK Banks Are Losing a Third of Their Market to Fintechs

A new Bain & Company analysis paints a stark picture for traditional UK banking. The consultancy found that incumbents have slipped from capturing 95% of addressable banking revenue in the early 2000s to approximately 80% today — and projects they'll hold only 65% by 2030. Fifteen percentage points of market share, transferred to challengers in less than a decade.

The consumer data supports it. Over 62% of UK banking customers now use at least one challenger bank. But the nuance matters: only 14% have fully abandoned traditional institutions, which means the competitive landscape is still contested, not settled.

The timing is deliberate. Bain published days after Revolut's full UK banking licence became operational, completing a four-year application process. With FSCS-protected deposits for 13 million customers and lending products now on the roadmap, Revolut moves from fintech challenger to regulated bank. The £3 billion UK investment commitment and 1,000 new jobs signal that Revolut sees the licence as a platform for credit, not just a regulatory badge.

For engineering leaders in banking: this isn't just a competitive story. It's a technology architecture story. The challengers winning market share are shipping on modern stacks — and the incumbents losing it are fighting their own infrastructure as much as their competitors.

Key Takeaways

  • Traditional UK banks have lost ~15 percentage points of addressable revenue to challengers — heading to 35% loss by 2030.
  • 62% of UK banking customers use at least one challenger, but only 14% have fully switched — the battle is far from over.
  • Revolut's full banking licence unlocks lending and credit — the real revenue engines that were locked behind regulation until now.
  • The competitive advantage is architectural: challengers ship on modern stacks while incumbents fight legacy infrastructure.

Sources: Bain & Co, Chris Skinner / The Finanser, FinTech Futures

The Leadership Read

Mike Cannon-Brookes on the AI Restructuring

'It would be disingenuous to pretend AI doesn't change the mix'

Atlassian's co-founder published an unusually candid blog post on March 11, the same day he laid off 1,600 people. Most CEO layoff letters are sterile affairs — passive voice, "difficult decisions," strategic euphemisms. Cannon-Brookes went further.

He described the restructuring as self-funding: the savings would be redirected into AI and enterprise sales, two areas where Atlassian has "high momentum." He acknowledged directly that AI changes both the skills needed and the number of roles required in certain areas. No hedging.

The most interesting detail was who was protected. Cannon-Brookes explicitly named three groups that were retained: high performers, people in critical-to-mission roles, and early-career employees and graduates. That last category is notable — it signals a bet on building the next generation of AI-native engineers rather than restructuring purely around short-term cost.

Over 900 of the 1,600 cuts were in R&D. The CTO was replaced by two co-CTOs. Jefferies estimated the cuts could boost Atlassian's operating margin by several points in FY2026-27. The stock, at $75 and down 53% year-to-date, barely reacted.

For engineering leaders, Cannon-Brookes's letter is worth reading because it says out loud what many are thinking: AI is changing team composition, and pretending otherwise is its own kind of failure.

Our approach is not 'AI replaces people.' But it would be disingenuous to pretend AI doesn't change the mix of skills we need or the number of roles required in certain areas. It does.

— Mike Cannon-Brookes, Atlassian CEO

Outside In

The SaaSpocalypse Through Goldman's Lens

Why the software market selloff is structural, not cyclical

Goldman Sachs analysis, covered across multiple financial outlets, predicts AI agents will significantly broaden the overall software market by the decade's end — but capture disproportionate profit share. The consultancy estimates that by 2030, over 60% of software economics may flow through agent-based systems rather than traditional SaaS licences.

The structural argument: intelligence is becoming mobile, data is becoming understandable, and workflows are becoming replicable. Software increasingly resembles a container, with intelligence becoming the actual product. The implication is that the recurring-revenue moats SaaS companies built — vendor lock-in through data formats, workflow integration, institutional knowledge — are eroding simultaneously.

Short sellers profited over $20 billion in 2026 betting against traditional SaaS. The IGV Software Index was down 30% from its September peak by February. What started as a valuation correction is now being read as a permanent repricing of business models.

Not everyone agrees. Wedbush's Dan Ives argues investors are making a mistake dumping software stocks, believing AI will complement rather than displace existing models. The reality is probably both — some categories (task management, CRM, tier-1 support) face existential pressure, while others (security, observability, infrastructure) may be enhanced by AI rather than replaced.
