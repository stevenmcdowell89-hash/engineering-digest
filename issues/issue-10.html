Jargon Watch PS25/12 & CASS 15

Policy Statement 25/12 is the FCA's published rulebook instrument that brought the Safeguarding Supplementary Regime into force on 7 May 2026. The "25" is the year of issue; "12" the sequence number. It amends the FCA Handbook to insert CASS 15 — a new chapter of the Client Assets sourcebook — alongside the pre-existing CASS 10A (the FCA chapter on payment and e-money safeguarding, now supplemented by CASS 15). The key change for engineering teams: what were principles-based expectations became enforceable rules with daily, monthly, and annual data-delivery obligations.

What went live on 7 May

As of 7 May 2026, the FCA's (Financial Conduct Authority, the UK's primary financial services regulator) Safeguarding Supplementary Regime is in force under PS25/12 for all authorised payment institutions — firms authorised by the FCA to provide payment services, distinct from a bank — and e-money institutions (EMIs — regulated firms licensed to issue electronic money, such as digital wallets and prepaid cards). The regime replaces the principles-based safeguarding framework of CASS 10A with the prescriptive obligations of the new CASS 15 chapter.

The headline change is not the existence of safeguarding — the requirement to ringfence customer funds in dedicated accounts, segregated from the firm's own money, so they can be returned if the firm fails — but the standard of evidence that safeguarding now demands: production-grade data infrastructure across four distinct obligations.

The four engineering obligations

First, D+1 reconciliation (a reconciliation run completed by the close of the business day following the reference date) is now a daily production requirement: internal reconciliation against the D+1 segregation requirement, and external reconciliation against third-party bank data, both must run every reconciliation day. Shortfalls must be remedied from the firm's own funds the same day.

Second, a dedicated monthly safeguarding return replaces the previous monthly questions — covering the D+1 segregation requirement, reconciliation completion status, and data on relevant assets and custodians. Third, most firms must arrange an annual CASS 15 audit under SUP 3A (the FCA Handbook chapter governing auditor appointment), with the first report due within six months of the period end. Fourth, firms must maintain a resolution pack — account maps, safeguarding records, and reconciliation logs — that is current and immediately usable by an insolvency practitioner, not a static document filed once.

The FCA expects an increase in adverse audit opinions in the short term, as greater scrutiny surfaces previously hidden deficiencies in safeguarding practices.

— Osborne Clarke analysis of PS25/12 (May 2026)

From process to production

The practical pressure is one of reliability, not existence. Most payment institutions and EMIs already run reconciliation processes — but those were built for internal reporting, tolerating batch delays and manual corrections invisible to an auditor. CASS 15 changes the test: the question is no longer whether reconciliation ran, but whether the output is accurate enough for FCA audit. Reconciliation must be treated as a first-class production system; the resolution pack as a data product rather than a document — its accuracy only as good as the pipelines that feed it.

What comes next

The FCA will review the regime's effectiveness after the first full audit period — firms that discover pipeline deficiencies after the first CASS 15 opinion is issued face remediation inside an active supervisory relationship, which is the harder path.

For teams building in the Middle East, the ADGM (Abu Dhabi Global Market — the international financial centre and free zone in Abu Dhabi) published AML/CFT (Anti-Money Laundering / Counter Financing of Terrorism) amendments also effective from May 1, creating a parallel regulatory two-front week for any engineering leadership spanning both UK and ME payment operations.

Key Takeaways
  • Daily reconciliation pipelines and resolution packs are now audit evidence — a team whose pipeline was good enough for internal reporting may not be good enough for CASS 15 scrutiny.
  • The first CASS 15 audit opinions are ~12 months away — the implementation window is narrow, and adverse opinions are the expected starting point, not an aberration.
  • Resolution packs are data products, not documents — their accuracy depends on the quality of the pipelines that feed them.

Sources: Osborne Clarke, FCA PS25/12, Bratby Law (May 2026)

Wise — Leading an observability re-architecture through ownership culture

When Marta Lima joined Wise's Observability domain, the team was mid-migration: rebuilding the entire observability stack — migrating to the LGTM stack (an open-source observability toolkit composed of Loki for log aggregation, Grafana for dashboarding, Tempo for distributed tracing, and Mimir for long-term metrics storage; maintained by Grafana Labs and widely used as an alternative to proprietary APM platforms) — concurrently with a Kubernetes platform migration (Kubernetes — open-source container orchestration system, the standard for running containerised applications across clusters), without interrupting the engineering organisation. The stack is built in-house — a deliberate choice reflecting Wise's requirement to handle high data volumes under strict regulatory constraints across multiple countries — and the migration took over a year. What made it tractable, Lima argues, was not technical planning alone but Wise's domain ownership culture: teams are fully accountable for their domains, with genuine authority to move fast when the data supports it. The platform team's role is to provide the underlying infrastructure that lets product engineers focus on product; the observability team's role is to give every engineer real-time visibility into their service's behaviour — which is the ability to understand the internal state of a system from its external outputs (logs, metrics, and traces), distinct from monitoring, which watches for predefined conditions. Neither depends on a central coordination layer to make decisions.

The transferable pattern for engineering leaders managing platform modernisation without dedicated SRE headcount is the accountability structure that came first: when a team owns the outcome end-to-end, concurrent migrations become tractable because coordination overhead is absorbed within the domain rather than escalated.

The takeaway: Wise's LGTM observability migration succeeded not because it was well-planned but because domain ownership meant the team absorbed the coordination complexity that would otherwise have required cross-org negotiation.

Valid provenance wasn't enough: Mini Shai-Hulud turns CI/CD trust into the attack surface

On 11 May 2026, TeamPCP (the threat-actor designation used by Wiz and security researchers for the group behind the Mini Shai-Hulud worm campaigns) exploited a pull_request_target misconfiguration — a GitHub Actions trigger that runs with write permissions even from an external fork's pull request — in TanStack's monorepo. The attacker used this to poison the pnpm build cache (pnpm is a fast Node.js package manager; cache poisoning here injected malicious content that subsequent builds fetched as legitimate), then extracted OIDC tokens (OpenID Connect — short-lived identity tokens used to authenticate GitHub Actions workflows against external services like the npm registry) directly from runner process memory. Those tokens were used to publish malicious packages to the npm (Node Package Manager registry — the primary JavaScript/TypeScript distribution channel) and PyPI ecosystems without ever stealing credentials.

The published packages carried valid SLSA Build Level 3 attestations and valid Sigstore signatures — SLSA (Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts, a Google-originated framework defining build-pipeline integrity levels) and Sigstore (an open-source toolkit for signing software supply chains using short-lived cryptographic certificates) both confirmed the pipeline ran correctly. They could not confirm the pipeline itself was the attack surface.

84 malicious artifacts across 42 @tanstack packages were published on May 11. The worm self-propagated to Mistral AI, UiPath, and over 160 other packages within hours. Environments that ran @tanstack packages on May 11 are treated as fully compromised in the incident response pattern: the remediation cycle involves rotating all accessible secrets (GitHub tokens, npm tokens, AWS credentials, Kubernetes service accounts, CI/CD secrets), removing persistence hooks in .claude/ and .vscode/ directories, and blocking C2 domains git-tanstack.com and *.getsession.org. An SBOM (Software Bill of Materials — a structured list of all components in a software build) with valid attestations offers no protection when the build system generating the attestations is the attack surface. Structural mitigations include auditing pull_request_target workflows, pinning GitHub Action versions to commit SHAs rather than tags, and applying GitHub's OIDC token scoping mitigations.

The takeaway: SLSA attestations and Sigstore signatures confirm that the build pipeline ran correctly — they cannot confirm the pipeline was not itself compromised; a provenance chain that starts at a compromised build environment is valid all the way down.

Sources: Wiz, TanStack postmortem, Snyk, StepSecurity (May 2026)

First-year ROI on AI-assisted development — 500-person org model

DORA gives AI-assisted development a CFO model — and warns about the J-curve

"The greatest returns on AI investment come not from the tools themselves but from a strategic focus on the underlying organizational system: the quality of the internal platform, the clarity of workflows, and the alignment of teams. Without this foundation, AI creates localized pockets of productivity that are often lost in downstream chaos." That is the central claim of the DORA ROI of AI-Assisted Software Development report — where DORA refers to the DevOps Research and Assessment team at Google Cloud, not the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (a different DORA in financial-services regulation context).

The illustrative model for a 500-person engineering organisation ($176k fully loaded salary) shows ~$11.6m first-year return against ~$8.4m investment: 39% ROI, ~8-month payback. The headline figure is less useful than the J-curve warning that accompanies it: a temporary productivity dip before long-term gains, caused by the learning curve, a "verification tax" from reviewing AI-generated code, and the need to adapt downstream processes like testing and change approval to handle increased code volumes. Leaders who mistake the dip for failure risk pulling funding during the period that generates the eventual return. An interactive calculator is available at dora.dev/ai/roi/report.

The takeaway: Engineering leaders now have a Google-branded financial model to take to a CFO conversation about AI tooling — and a specific term ("the J-curve") for the temporary dip that finance teams will otherwise treat as evidence the investment isn't working.

Sources: InfoQ, DORA report, Google Cloud (May 2026)

In Practice Calibration & performance conversations

Engineering performance calibration as decision-making infrastructure

Pensero editorial team · Pensero, April 2026 · Read the full piece

Calibration sessions often function as opinion-reconciliation meetings — managers arrive with different standards, negotiate toward rough consensus, and leave with decisions that are difficult to revisit later. The specific framework this piece argues for reframes calibration as decision-making infrastructure rather than a calendar ritual: a structured process with a defined data layer, a two-tier conversation model, and explicit handling of variables that current levelling rubrics do not address.

The data layer rests on complexity-weighted delivery metrics (metrics that weight engineering output by the estimated complexity of the work, rather than counting story points or commits — which prevents large but simple work from distorting comparisons). The specific metrics proposed: delivery per headcount, defect rate, AI adoption share, roadmap alignment, cycle time, capitalizable delivery (work that qualifies as capital expenditure under accounting rules, relevant for teams whose CFO tracks software development cost categories), and knowledge gaps. The key structural argument is that VP and CTO-level calibration — team-to-team comparison for resource allocation — requires different data than EM-level calibration, which is about individual promotion evidence. Conflating the two produces sessions where neither conversation happens well.

The 2026-specific variable the piece introduces is agent-authored delivery (code or output produced by an AI coding agent rather than directly by a human engineer). Most levelling rubrics were designed around code-output signals — PRs merged, velocity, lines of code — that do not yet distinguish human from agent contribution. An engineer whose throughput has increased significantly this quarter may be demonstrating either mastery of AI-augmented workflows or a dependency on agent output that lacks the signal quality the framework was designed to measure. Calibration sessions in Q2 2026 will encounter this split for the first time, often without explicit data to resolve it.

Calibration works when the data reflects the work, not just the volume of output. The agent-authored delivery variable is the first place where this distinction becomes visible in the numbers.

The concrete mechanism the piece proposes is treating agent-authored delivery share as a new column in the calibration data view — surfacing which engineers are extending AI-augmented capability in ways that compound their judgement, and which are generating throughput that looks like productivity but reflects authorship volume rather than decision quality. Without this column, calibration sessions reward the latter as readily as the former.

What to try One anchor for the next calibration session: before the meeting, ask whether the data on the table distinguishes between team-to-team comparison and individual promotion evidence — and, if anyone on the team has meaningfully shifted toward agent-augmented delivery this cycle, whether that shift is visible in the metrics or invisible beneath a throughput number.
The Leadership Read

Agentic coding is making engineering-management skills part of the IC job

Sage Lazzaro's piece in LeadDev is not primarily about AI. It is about what happens to role boundaries, levelling frameworks, and career paths when the competencies that engineering managers have spent years developing — breaking down problems, delegating, setting context, managing toward outcomes — become the core skill of an IC (individual contributor — an engineer without direct reports, whose primary work is technical execution) working with AI agents.

The boundary collapse

The specific argument is that agentic coding (the practice of using AI coding agents — tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, or Claude Code — to plan, implement, test, and in some cases merge code with reduced or restructured human oversight) is collapsing the IC/EM (engineering manager — an engineer with direct reports, responsible for people management, team direction, and delivery coordination) boundary from both directions simultaneously. ICs managing five AI agents in parallel need to prioritise tasks, set context, review outputs, and manage toward a delivery outcome — structurally the same work as managing a team of junior engineers. Meanwhile, experienced EMs are being actively recruited as senior ICs at agent-first companies, because their skill set maps directly to orchestrating agent workflows.

Scott Breitenother, co-founder and CEO of Kilo — an agentic coding startup that launched in early 2025 — says: "Instead of having teams of humans working together, each individual contributor ends up being like a manager. But instead of a manager of humans, they're a manager of agents." Every engineer Kilo has hired was previously either an engineering manager or a senior peer mentor. Breitenother's hypothesis is that the adjustment from managing humans to managing agents is smaller than the adjustment from managing no-one to managing agents: the delegation and context-setting muscle is already there.

Where levelling frameworks are misaligned

The second concrete mechanism is the mismatch between how current levelling frameworks measure performance and what the work now requires. Levelling rubrics built on code-output signals — PRs merged, velocity, lines of code delivered — reward throughput. An IC managing multiple agents can generate high throughput while the work that actually distinguishes senior contribution (context quality, delegation decisions, output review, delivery management) is invisible in the metrics. Vinay Perneti, VP of Engineering at Augment Code, puts it plainly: "Instead, I think you really want to embrace outcomes."

The data the article cites is pointed: staff and principal engineers now regularly out-earn engineering managers by 15 to 25% at top companies, according to IT hiring firm HR Oasis. Google has cut 35% of managers overseeing fewer than three people. Amazon required every team to increase its ratio of ICs to managers by 15% by Q1 2025. These are separate decisions taken by separate organisations, but the direction is consistent: the management layer is contracting while the IC layer is expected to absorb more of what was previously management work.

The career-path implication

For engineering leaders, the useful tension here is not that "AI changes everything." It is that careers and team structures are shifting before the levelling frameworks that govern them have caught up. An engineer who is demonstrably excellent at managing agent workflows may be measured — and paid, and promoted — against a rubric designed for a world where that skill did not exist. That gap will surface in calibration sessions, in retention conversations, and in hiring decisions. Recognising it explicitly is more useful than waiting for the frameworks to self-update.

This could be worth talking about: If your levelling rubrics were redesigned today, would they reward the quality of delegation and outcome-management decisions — or would they still reward throughput, with agent-assisted throughput looking indistinguishable from human-authored throughput?
