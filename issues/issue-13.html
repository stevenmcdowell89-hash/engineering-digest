Foreword

Agents became a money question this week — on both sides of the org chart. Outside it, Visa and Mastercard are preparing to let software agents move money on their card rails. Inside it, the cost of coding models and the economics of how many people one manager can hold are reshaping budgets and team shapes at once. The through-line is the same: someone now has to own what an agent, or a stretched manager, is authorised to do.

Wider Engineering Landscape

Agent-initiated card payments move from pilot to live rails this month (3 min)

Jargon Watch The agent-commerce stack

Trusted Agent Protocol (TAP): Visa's open framework, announced September 2025, for an AI agent and a merchant to exchange cryptographically signed messages during checkout.

Verified Agent ID: a registry entry that lets a merchant confirm an agent is who it claims to be against Visa's public keys.

Intelligent Commerce Connect: Visa's productised version of all this for developers, with general availability targeted for June 2026.

Agent Pay: Mastercard's equivalent, already running live transactions in several markets.

Tokenisation: replacing a real card number with a single-use token so the agent never holds the actual card. Visa Developer →

The card networks that UK and Middle East banks settle on are about to let software act as the cardholder. Visa and Mastercard are both moving agent-initiated card payments — transactions where a production agent with payment-action authority can complete a purchase without a human approving each step, not the assisted "agent-mode" buttons inside a developer tool — from controlled pilots toward live rails. This is the world the loyalty and commerce-media patch operates in shifting underneath it, not a shipped product to adopt this week.

What is actually new this week

Visa first announced its Trusted Agent Protocol in September 2025. The in-window news is the general-availability window opening: Visa's developer-facing Intelligent Commerce Connect, unveiled 8 April 2026, remains in pilot with GA targeted for June 2026 — turning on this month, not already shipped. Mastercard's Agent Pay has gone further into live transactions in several markets, though without a single confirmed GA date. The reaction this week is about the rails going from demo to default.

Who signs what

The protocols differ in what the agent is actually authorised to assert — its intent, a bounded cart mandate, or a fully delegated payment. That distinction is where the engineering and trust questions live.

ProtocolWhat the agent asserts
Visa TAP + Verified Agent IDSigned identity + intent at checkout; merchant verifies against Visa keys
Mastercard Agent PayTokenised credential for live agent-initiated transactions
Stripe MPP (Issues 11–12)Order intent passed agent→merchant; payment stays on existing rails
Google AP2 / UCPCart mandate signed by the user, carried by the agent

The question is no longer whether agents will pay — it is whose keys vouch for them when they do.

— Engineering Digest, on the June GA window

Why it lands on the loyalty patch

Once an agent can hold a verified identity and a tokenised credential, the surfaces that sit on top of card rails — rewards, points accrual, commerce-media offers — inherit a new kind of actor. The leadership conversation is about which products in the loyalty and commerce-media stack assume a human is at the keyboard, and what verifying an agent's authority does to that assumption. The rails are turning on this month; the design questions arrive with them.

Two Stories Worth Knowing

Management & teams
Concept of the week Span of control

The number of people who report directly to one manager. A small span (4–6) leaves room for coaching and one-to-ones; a wide span (12+) turns the role into triage and routing. It is the single lever most companies are pulling when they "flatten."

12.1
Average direct reports per manager, 2025

The megamanager surge is quietly rewriting the EM job

The average manager now oversees 12.1 direct reports, up from 10.9 in 2024 and 8.2 when Gallup first measured in 2013. Meta's applied-AI engineering division has pushed the logic to its extreme at a 50:1 ratio — a data point that follows directly from the reset we covered in Issue 11. Analysis this week frames it as the rise of the "megamanager": flattening pitched as efficiency, with the costs only starting to show.

The mechanism is simple and unforgiving. Past roughly a dozen reports, the work that defines management — calibration, growth conversations, knowing who is struggling before they say so — gets squeezed out by sheer coordination load. The same debate ran through Issue 12's Coinbase "no pure managers" model, where managers were expected to keep shipping; widening spans bakes that expectation in by default.

The takeaway: span of control has become the quiet variable behind "flatter org" decisions, and at the top of the range the EM role changes character — engineering leaders are now setting spans that decide whether their managers can still manage.

Sources: Pinnacle, Gallup

10×
Cost-efficiency claim vs GPT-5.5

Microsoft ships its own coding models at Build

At its Build conference on 2 June, Microsoft unveiled a family of seven in-house MAI models — among them MAI-Code-1-Flash, a small 5-billion-parameter coding model now rolling into VS Code and GitHub Copilot, and MAI-Thinking-1, a reasoning model (one that spends extra compute "thinking" before answering). The framing was self-sufficiency: cut the dependence on OpenAI and the per-token cost that comes with it. AI chief Mustafa Suleyman claimed up to 10× better cost efficiency than GPT-5.5 on refined tasks.

The timing matters for budgets. It lands the same week GitHub Copilot's usage-based billing took effect on 1 June — the 10×–50× bill stories and new pooled AI-credit model we covered in Issue 12 — making model mix a live cost lever that leaders now actively manage.

The takeaway: the coding-model market is fragmenting along price, and which models sit behind a team's tooling is now a budget decision leaders own directly.

What changes when a director's org crosses a hundred engineers

Thiago Ghisi · "Scaling to 100+ as a Director," QCon London 2025 (InfoQ) · Read the full piece

When Thiago Ghisi's organisation at Nubank crossed a hundred engineers, the job stopped being about his own teams and became about the teams he could no longer see directly. His QCon London 2025 talk offers a frame for that shift, which he calls the "Three Levels of Impact" — the idea that a director operates simultaneously across their own org, the skip-level organisation beneath their managers, and the company as a whole, and that influence has to be earned in that order.

Level 1The director's own org — prove changes here first
Level 2Skip-level — the teams beneath the managers
Level 3The company — peers as a first team

His sequencing rule is concrete: understand the wider company outside-in, but always act inside-out — prove a change works in the director's own org before pushing it upward. The most useful operating principle for the megamanager era running through this issue is what he refuses to delegate. A director should delegate real authority and decision rights to their managers, while never delegating away their own awareness of who the top and bottom performers are across the skip-level. That awareness is what a wide span of control quietly erodes.

Manage the peaks and the valleys — across the whole organisation, not just your direct reports.

At scale, a director's leverage shifts from doing the work to moving talented people across boundaries for company priorities, rather than hoarding them inside a "kingdom." Treating peer directors as a first team is what makes that possible.

What to try For directors whose spans have widened past the point of direct visibility, the open question is which signals about performance and morale they still see first-hand at the skip-level — and which ones now only reach them second-hand, after they have already become a problem.

Outside In

Vercel

VE
Vercel Design Engineering Glenn Hitchcock, Henry Heffernan, Rauno Freiberg & others · Vercel "Design Engineering at Vercel"
Read the post →

"Iterate to Greatness" is one of Vercel's stated principles, and the team frames it as a way to balance business goals with craft by shipping continuous improvements and avoiding the perfection trap. That ethos sits behind a structural choice many engineering orgs are circling: making design engineering a first-class discipline in its own right.

Collapsing the design-to-frontend handoff

Vercel's account describes Design Engineers as people who pair aesthetic judgement with the technical skill to ship polished solutions themselves — owning a problem end to end rather than passing a mockup over a wall to a frontend engineer. They work in three modes: collaborating with designers, embedding in product teams, and independently owning features that address real user pain.

The transferable lesson is structural, not aesthetic: a large engineering org collapsed the design-to-frontend handoff by treating the hybrid design-engineer as a first-class role instead of a coordination problem between two functions. The handoff that usually loses fidelity simply stops existing.

Why it travels

Swap "Vercel" for any large engineering org and the lesson holds: the friction in shipping polished product is often the seam between who designs and who builds. Naming a role that owns both sides removes the seam. For teams in the loyalty and commerce-media space, where the surface a customer touches is the product, that seam is expensive — and the fix is a hiring and team-shape decision more than a tooling one.

The takeaway: making design engineering a first-class role is a structural lever for shipping quality faster, and it lands as a hiring and team-shape decision that leaders own directly.

The Leadership Read

The middle-manager cuts saving millions today build the empty pipeline of 2028 (4 min)

KT
Kristien Turner · Fortune 12 April 2026
Read on Fortune →

Only 6% of Gen Z say they want a senior leadership role, according to Deloitte research cited in a Fortune piece by Kristien Turner published on 12 April 2026. The figure has resurfaced this week as the span-of-control story gains fresh traction — and it pairs with the megamanager coverage above as the slower, structural counterweight. The argument was made in April; what is new is the audience now reacting to it.

The deferred-cost thesis

Turner's contention is that the middle-management cuts booking immediate savings are quietly dismantling the layer that produces the next generation of leaders. Gartner projects one in five companies will eliminate more than half their middle managers by the end of this year. The efficiency is real and immediate. The cost, the piece argues, lands in 2028, when the senior roles those managers would have grown into sit empty.

The practical learning — where someone makes a bad decision and their manager walks them through why it failed? That's gone.

— Kristien Turner, Fortune

Where leaders were actually made

The mechanism the article names is informal coaching. The development of future leaders did not happen in a programme; it happened in the conversation after a bad call, in the translation of what the CEO meant into what a team should actually do. Flatten the layer that held those conversations and the 6% figure reads as a rational response: this generation watched the management track get treated as expendable and drew the obvious conclusion. The two trends compound — wider spans remove the time to coach, and the cuts remove the coaches.

Read the full article: fortune.com →

This could be worth talking about: Engineering leaders flattening their orgs now face the question of where their 2028 senior engineers and directors will come from — and whether the coaching that produces them survives a widening span of control, or quietly disappears with the managers being cut.
